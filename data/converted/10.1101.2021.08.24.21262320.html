Abstract

Background Gliomas present a formidable challenge for translational progress. Cancer metabolism is of increasing interest to understand candidate therapeutic vulnerabilities, but few methods exist to evaluate live human malignancies. We sought to characterize the global extracellular metabolome of diverse human gliomas during standard-of-care craniotomies.

Methods We utilized high molecular weight (HMW) microdialysis with elevated flow rate pumps under an investigational device exemption to sample the microenvironments of fourteen live human gliomas and adjacent brain during fifteen neurosurgical resections. The global signature of recovered microdialysate was evaluated via ultra-performance liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-MS/MS); enrichment and correlation analyses were utilized to identify reproducible tumor phenotypes across patients in discovery and validation cohorts.

Results 162 named metabolites were identified in at least 40 of 44 catheters from 20 μL of microdialysate representing 10 minutes of intra-operative collection time. Rank-based and correlation analyses highlighted metabolic heterogeneity across and between patients, as well as marked metabolic convergence across the contrast-enhancing regions of molecularly diverse tumors. Guanidinoacetate (GAA) demonstrated the highest differential abundance between contrast-enhancing tumor and brain adjacent to tumor.

Conclusions A reproducible extracellular metabolic signature defines the contrast-enhancing region of molecularly diverse astrocytomas, as compared to each patient’s adjacent brain.

Introduction

Diffuse infiltrative gliomas, including astrocytomas and oligodendrogliomas, comprise most adult primary malignant brain tumors and remain incurable, regardless of the best available therapies 1 . Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) mutations occur in all oligodendrogliomas and a subset of astrocytomas, affording improved prognosis due to typically slower growth and more favorable response to standard-of-care radiation and alkylating chemotherapy 2 , 3 . Highlighting the dismal rate of therapeutic progress, the last drug found to improve survival for gliomas was temozolomide in 2005 1 . Glioma heterogeneity among patients hampers therapeutic generalizations across diverse patient cohorts. Moreover, profoundly diverse genetic, epigenetic, and metabolic phenotypes accumulate within individual patients’ tumor ecosystems 4 , 5 . Such heterogeneity has hampered therapeutic efforts to target glioma-associated mutations. Conversely, divergent gliomas may nonetheless leverage convergent metabolic survival strategies in the setting of nutrient deprivation, genotoxic stress, and growth requirements common across tumors 6 , 7 . As such, several features of glioma metabolism are increasingly viewed as promising therapeutic targets. Few strategies currently exist to characterize the metabolic landscape of human gliomas in situ.

Microdialysis has been used to quantify human extracellular biomarkers of traumatic and hypoxic brain injury in neurocritical care units. Microdialysis is also a well-established method to quantify central nervous system (CNS) drug delivery in early phase clinical trials 8 - 10 and has been used longitudinally to study metabolites present in human gliomas when compared to adjacent brain 11 , 12 . However, while some studies have utilized intraoperative microdialysis to enable quantification of select extracellular metabolites 13 - 15 , no study thus far has deployed intra-operative microdialysis to characterize the global extracellular glioma metabolome. To that end, we initiated a pilot feasibility study leveraging high molecular weight (HMW) microdialysis catheters in 5 surgeries, followed by validation in 10 additional surgeries to characterize the global extracellular metabolome of glioma across multiple regions of diverse gliomas. Our findings provide evidence for both divergent and convergent metabolism within and between live human brain tumors and suggest that IDH status alone is insufficient to predict the global extracellular metabolic signature of live human gliomas.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Patient Cohort, Study Design, and Intraoperative Microdialysis

All study procedures were approved by the Mayo Clinic Institutional Review Board (IRB). Patients provided written informed consent to participate in NCT04047264 --an ongoing study evaluating the safety and feasibility of intraoperative high molecular weight (100 kDa) microdialysis under an investigational device exemption. Study eligibility includes adults (>18yo) undergoing a clinically indicated surgery for known or suspected glioma. Each patient (n=14; 15 surgeries; Fig. 1 ) underwent intraoperative microdialysis using up to three M-dialysis 100 kDA catheters and 107 variable rate microdialysis pumps under an investigational device exemption across radiographically diverse regions ( Fig. 2A-B ). See supplemental methods for details of microdialysis, pathology, and molecular tumor analyses.

Targeted Analysis of D/L-2-HG

Targeted metabolomic analysis of microdialysate was performed by the Mayo Clinic metabolomic core facility. L and D isomers of 2-hydroxyglutaratic acid were derivatized and quantified by liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (LC/MS) using slight modifications to previously described methods 16 - 18 . See the Supplemental Methods for details.

Untargeted Metabolomic Analysis

Untargeted metabolomic analysis was performed by Metabolon, Inc. Ultra-performance liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-MS/MS), which combines physical separation of liquid chromatography with the mass analysis capabilities of mass spectrometry. See the Supplemental Methods for details.

Ranked Metabolite lists

Ranked metabolite lists of tumor (Catheter X or Y) versus brain (Catheter Z) were generated from fifteen cases based on calculated fold changes of the peak area data (X/Z or Y/Z for the metabolite). This could not be done for patient Oligo 2 given the lack of brain-sampling catheter. In a discovery cohort, to determine an overall enhancing tumor-to-brain ranking for each metabolite, each patient’s tumor-to-brain metabolites were ranked based on fold change (X/Z) and then averaged across all three patients for that metabolite. In the validation cohort, the individual tumor-to-brain rankings were compared against the discovery cohort enhancing tumor-to-brain ranking associated with each metabolite.

Enrichment Analysis

Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) is the most widely utilized analytical method for rank-based analysis of data sets in biomedicine. GSEA is frequently performed using curated gene sets from the molecular signatures database (MSigDB), though custom feature sets can be used enabling enrichment analysis of metabolites rather than genes. Ranked metabolite lists were created as described above based on fold change to create metabolite libraries (.gmx file) against which these ranked lists (.rnk files) were compared to determine relative positive and negative enrichment ( Supplemental Data ). Additionally, results from Björkblom et al. were included as an external reference 19 . The number of feature sets evaluated is included in calculations to generate a normalized enrichment score and FDR for each.

MetaboAnalyst

Metabolomic analyses were performed using MetaboAnalyst (5.0), a publicly available web-based tool to analyze and visualize metabolomic data 20 . The normalized peak area data for the metabolites present across at least 40/44 catheters were entered into MetaboAnalyst without any further filtering. Assuming a monotonical rather than linear relation between metabolites and samples, Spearman Correlation maps were generated in MetaboAnalyst based on features (metabolites present across all samples) and samples.

Statistical Analysis

Data are presented as normalized peak areas (median = 1 for each metabolite across batches) provided from Metabolon Inc. and are available in Supplemental File 1. MetaboAnalyst 5.0 was used for Spearman correlations. Enrichment Analysis was performed using GSEA 4.1.0 (Gene Set Enrichment Analysis), repurposed for metabolite set analysis using custom feature sets. Normality of metabolites in Figure 4 was tested via D’Agostino-Pearson test; a Wilcoxon signed rank test for non-parametric distributions was then performed on paired enhancing tumor and brain metabolites. Graphs were generated using GraphPad PRISM 9.1. FDR <0.05 was considered statistically significant for enrichment analysis.

RESULTS

Elevated D-2-Hydroxyglutarate (D-2-HG) in IDH-mutant tumor microdialysate

D-2-HG is markedly elevated in IDH-mutant gliomas and remains one of the best-characterized glioma oncometabolites 21 . We initiated our intra-operative microdialysis trial in a discovery cohort of five patients, three of whom had IDH-mutated gliomas (Oligo 2 , Oligo 3 1, and Astro 4-mut 1) and two of whom had GBMs (GBM WT 1, GBM WT 2) ( Fig. 1 ). To determine if D-2-HG levels in microdialysate quantitative reflect IDH status, we utilized 10 μL of a 40 μL microdialysate sample to quantify D-2-HG via targeted metabolomic analysis. D-2-HG was elevated in microdialysate from IDH-mutant as compared to IDH-wild-Type (WT) tumors (median: 27.29 versus 0.63 μM) ( Supplementary Fig. S1A ). Patient Astro 4-mut 1 showed a >1000x difference in D-2-HG between non-enhancing tumor (FLAIR) and adjacent brain infiltrated by tumor (831.80 versus 0.68 μM). These data demonstrated the feasibility of intraoperative HMW microdialysis to quantify a dialyzable oncometabolite during standard-of-care surgery for tumor resection.

Untargeted metabolomics is increasingly leveraged to gain more comprehensive insights regarding the global metabolome. We utilized 20 μL of microdialysate from each catheter for untargeted metabolomic analysis via the Metabolon platform 22 . While low volumes of microdialysate precluded validation of all metabolites measured by untargeted metabolomics, enough volume was available to validate D-2-HG across untargeted and targeted metabolomic platforms. Untargeted analysis reported total 2-HG. In the discovery cohort, these were plotted against the combined stereoisomer (D-2-HG + L-2-HG) levels from targeted analyses yielding a robust correlation (R 2 =0.9989) between the two platforms across a 2,500-fold concentration range ( Supplementary Fig. S1B) , suggesting feasible quantitative comparisons from untargeted metabolomic data. Having established a strong correlation between untargeted and targeted metabolomic analyses, evaluation of the 2-HG peak area in the next cohort of 10 cases, including GBM WT 2’s repeat resection (GBM WT 2B) confirmed increased levels in IDH-mutant tumors when compared to IDH-WT tumors (21.41x average normalized peak area in IDH-mutant tumor compared to IDH-WT) ( Fig. 2C ). In line with other studies reporting occasional false-positive detection of 2-HG in IDH-WT tumors 23 , one patient with an H3K27M-mutated infiltrative grade 4 astrocytoma demonstrated elevated 2-HG levels despite their next generation sequencing-confirming IDH-WT status. These data suggest that some IDH-WT tumors may produce elevated 2-HG levels via non-IDH-related mechanisms.

Global metabolomics reveals a dynamic glioma-versus-brain environment

To ensure comparison and reproducibility of metabolite levels across patients, we analyzed 162 named metabolites that were present in at least 90% of the catheters ( > 40/44 catheters). Intra-operatively, catheters were placed in radiographically distinct areas of the tumor (enhancing, non-enhancing (“FLAIR”), and/or infiltrated brain). Due to the diffuse nature of patient Oligo 2 ’s tumor, only non-enhancing tumor could be sampled.

Prior work by Björkblom et al. 24 reported a metabolic phenotype of glioblastoma microdialysate compared to that of adjacent brain. However, these studies were performed over several days, whereas our samples were collected acutely during surgery. As such, we first asked if acute intraoperative sampling of the in vivo global metabolome via microdialysis could yield comparable results to that of prolonged catheter placements. We compared the fold changes to results from Björkblom et al, for their 24 significantly tumor- or brain-associated metabolites that were present in all of our catheters. Obvious discrepancies were only noted in 1 of 24 metabolites, 3-amino-2-piperidone (Supplementary Fig. S2) , demonstrating that 10 minutes of intraoperative sampling could reproduce previously identified glioma metabolic signatures from post-operative microdialysis.

Individualized signatures of enhancing tumor versus brain enriched across patients

Having demonstrated that our data appeared consistent with prior literature, we then proceeded to evaluate the full set of metabolites detected in at least 90% of catheters. For each patient, we ranked metabolites based on the fold change in peak area between enhancing (E) or non-enhancing tumor (NE), and brain adjacent to tumor (B) ( Fig. 1 ). Despite substantial variation between patients in the relative magnitude of metabolite fold-changes between tumor and brain ( Supplementary Fig. S3) , enhancing tumor- and brain-associated metabolites were consistent across patients in the discovery cohort ( Fig. 3A ) . This ranked order of metabolites was similarly observed in an independent validation cohort of patients with enhancing gliomas ( Fig. 3A ) . Top tumor-associated metabolic pathways included amino acid-associated metabolic pathways (KEGG), including leucine, isoleucine, and valine metabolism, in addition to carnitine metabolism. Brain-associated metabolic pathways included aminosugar, pentose, and alanine and aspartate metabolism.

To more rigorously and quantitatively compare metabolic phenotypes between catheters and between patients, we utilized enrichment analysis, to statistically identify similarities between and across patients’ catheters. Although generally applied to gene-based analyses, we repurposed this for metabolomic analysis. Using EA, we selected the top and bottom 35 metabolites based on fold change from each comparison and determined where these metabolites fell in the ranked metabolite lists for the remaining patients ( Supplemental Data ). In patients with enhancing astrocytomas, each patient’s individual tumor signature was robustly and positively enriched in the ranked list of enhancing tumor versus brain metabolites from other patients ( Fig. 3B ) . Conversely, the tumor-infiltrated brain signatures were negatively enriched in these same patient-specific tumor versus brain metabolite lists, consistent with a conserved metabolic signature of tumor versus infiltrated brain across patients ( Fig. 3C ) . Leveraging these individual patient-level data, metabolite enrichment between patients proved highly robust with a False Discovery Rate (FDR) of 0.000 for most interpatient comparisons ( Fig. 3D ) of enhancing tumor versus brain metabolites. For independent reference, the previously published metabolic signature of tumor versus adjacent brain from Björkblom et al 19 was evaluated and found to be strongly enriched at FDR of 0.002 or less for each of the enhancing astrocytomas ( Fig 3D ) . We also asked if the signature of enhancing and non-enhancing catheters is similar, utilizing enrichment analysis. Although similarities to non-enhancing catheters were observed for a subset of enhancing tumors (GBM WT 1, GBM WT 4, and H3K27M-Astro 4-WT ), most comparisons between enhancing and non-enhancing catheters revealed no significant enrichment ( Supplementary Fig. S4A) .

Metabolites of the enhancing glioma extracellular microenvironment

After demonstrating that the discovery and validation cohorts revealed a conserved metabolic signature in enhancing astrocytomas via two orthogonal methods, we then asked which metabolites defined the extracellular microenvironment across the full cohort of all 9 surgeries, two of which comprised patient GBM WT 2’s primary and recurrent tumor. Using a Wilcoxon rank sum test for non-parametric distributions, paired enhancing vs. brain catheters revealed 39 elevated metabolites in the enhancing tumor catheter and 22 elevated metabolites in the brain catheter using cutoff criteria of p-value < 0.05 and FC > 2 ( Fig. 4A ) . The top enhancing astrocytoma metabolite was guanidinoacetate (GAA) (FC (E/B) = 126.32x; p=0.0039), which, to our knowledge, has not previously been reported in the context of glioma ( Fig. 4B ) . The enhancing glioma signature was also defined by greater relative abundance of proline (FC=10.1x), carnitine-family metabolites (deoxycarnitine, carnitine, and acetylcarnitine at FC= 8.71x, 7.65x, and 7.47x, respectively), and N6-methyllysine (FC=7.17x) (all at p=0.0039).

Enhancing vs. non-enhancing glioma metabolic signatures

To our knowledge, the extracellular metabolic signature of non-enhancing gliomas has not been previously reported. In 8 of 9 enhancing cases, one catheter was placed in non-enhancing (T2 hyperintense) tumor. Our cohort also included individuals with entirely non-enhancing tumors, including four oligodendrogliomas and a molecular GBM. One enhancing tumor (gemistocytic astrocytoma WHO) was very firm preventing catheter placement in the enhancing portion, thus 2 non-enhancing locations were sampled. To understand the metabolome of non-enhancing glioma, we again utilized enrichment analysis to determine if we could identify a reproducible metabolic signature across non-enhancing catheters. Non-enhancing catheters from patients with enhancing tumors tended to be enriched for each other, as well as for the signature of enhancing catheters. Conversely, catheters from entirely non-enhancing tumors were rarely enriched for each other or for the enhancing tumor signature, with exception of one catheter from Oligo 3 1. ( Supplementary Fig. S4B) . This could suggest either 1) conserved glioma metabolism between enhancing and non-enhancing regions of contrast-enhancing gliomas or 2) diffusion of enhancing-region metabolites into areas of non-enhancing tumors.

To determine how non-enhancing glioma metabolism compared to the enhancing portion of the tumor, we utilized non-enhancing vs. enhancing ranked lists from our 8 patients with paired enhancing and non-enhancing catheters. Enrichment analyses revealed that the non-enhancing vs. enhancing portion of the glioma was significantly enriched for the brain metabolome across patients (FDR<0.004 for most patients) ( Supplementary Fig. S4C) . This suggests that non-enhancing portion of enhancing tumors has a metabolic signature that is intermediate between enhancing tumor and brain. In summary, intraoperative microdialysis evealed a conserved global tumor versus brain signature in extracellular fluid, particularly for enhancing astrocytomas. Importantly, rank-based analyses empowered statistical comparisons of individual patients’ glioma metabolomes to each other.

Metabolite and patient correlation analyses

Individualized intra-patient catheter comparisons were utilized for most analyses of our intraoperative microdialysis samples. Having now identified a conserved metabolic signature across enhancing astrocytomas, with some enrichment in the non-enhancing catheters of those tumors, we asked how patients’ catheters would correlate and cluster across all samples. As expected based on our robust metabolic signature, Spearman correlation of all 44 catheters revealed clustering of the enhancing grade 4 astrocytoma catheters ( Fig. 5A ). Interestingly, catheters from patients with oligodendrogliomas clustered based on the patient’s identity. Clustering of non-enhancing astrocytoma catheters was variable, but those clustering with the enhancing catheters (Astro 4-mut 1, Astro 4-mut 2, Astro 4-mut 3, and GemAstro 3-mut ) tended to have enrichment for enhancing catheters in GSEA ( Supplementary Fig. S4B) . We separately performed Spearman correlation considering only the catheters of patients with enhancing grade 4 astrocytomas. Results again confirmed clustering of most enhancing catheters, except for GBM WT 1 which clustered with non-enhancing catheters. Interestingly, the tumor of our one patient for whom microdialysis was performed twice (primary and recurrent: GBM WT 2 and 2B, respectively) clustered next to each other in both analyses. In general, most non-enhancing catheters tended to cluster together, although some did cluster with the brain catheters (H3K27M-Astro 4-WT Fig. 5B ) . Surprisingly, no obvious clustering by IDH status or recurrence status was observed. Spearman correlation of the 162 metabolites again suggested covariance of enhancing catheter-associated amino acid and carnitine metabolism. Brain catheter-associated metabolites also clustered together ( Supplementary Fig. S5) . In sum, results of correlation analyses suggest that the extracellular metabolome of molecularly diverse enhancing astrocytoma catheters clustered by region, whereas that of oligodendroglioma catheters clustered by patient. Furthermore, non-enhancing glioma metabolism may exist on a spectrum between enhancing tumor and brain metabolism based on the variable clustering of these catheters with enhancing or brain-sampling catheters.

DISCUSSION

We tested the feasibility of intraoperative microdialysis to acutely evaluate the extracellular metabolome within live human gliomas immediately prior to resection. This rarely leveraged window of opportunity within a standard-of-care glioma resection demonstrated: 1) robustly elevated D-2-HG in IDH-mutant glioma microdialysate; 2) metabolic features that reproducibly separate enhancing tumor from brain across molecularly and clinically diverse astrocytomas; and 3) lack of clustering by IDH-status or recurrence status.

Acute intraoperative microdialysis in a practical timeframe yielded relative levels of hundreds of metabolites for targeted and untargeted metabolomics analyses in fourteen patients with molecularly clinically diverse gliomas, including one patient’s primary and recurrent tumor. Increasing preclinical evidence suggests the potential therapeutic utility of targeting cancer-associated metabolic vulnerabilities. In contrast to the profound genomic heterogeneity seen within and across malignancies, a more finite set of convergent metabolic strategies might be deployed to meet the unavoidable bioenergetic demands of tumor metabolism 25 in the face of genomic instability 26 , 27 , oxidative stress 28 , hypoxia 29 , and nutrient deprivation 30 , 31 . Microdialysis surprisingly revealed a highly conserved and reproducible tumor metabolic signature of enhancing grade 4 astrocytomas whose samples differed across multiple clinical and technical parameters ( Fig. 1 ; Supplemental Table 1 ). Moreover, our rank-based analyses confirmed a set of glioma versus brain-associated metabolites identified in a previously published study of post-operative microdialysis ( Supplementary Fig. S2; Fig 3D ; 19 ). Technical methodology in the post-operative study varied significantly from ours and was performed by an independent team on a different continent confirming the robust nature of the enhancing glioma metabolome. Such interpatient and interstudy reproducibility in the enhancing tumor-versus-brain extracellular metabolome could suggest therapeutically targetable metabolic convergence across primary and recurrent IDH-mutant and IDH-wildtype astrocytomas.

IDH-mutant gliomas epitomize the oncogenic relevance of altered metabolism. We found elevated D-2-HG in the microdialysate in IDH-mutant tumors ( Supplementary Fig. 1; Fig. 2C ). D-2-HG was previously used as a pharmacodynamic biomarker for IDH inhibitors in tissue 32 and via magnetic resonance spectroscopy 33 - 35 . Our observations suggest that monitoring of extracellular D-2-HG via microdialysis could provide an additional tool to augment early phase translation of novel therapies. Interestingly, D-2-HG was higher in the non-enhancing than enhancing tumor in 3/3 patients with catheters in both regions of enhancing IDH-mutant tumors, suggesting that some tumor metabolites may either be lost down a disrupted blood brain barrier or may be produced at lower levels in relatively more hypoxic tumor regions.

Importantly, although IDH-mutations are thought to drive early tumorigenic metabolic and epigenetic changes, the global extracellular metabolome of enhancing astrocytoma was highly reproducible across all analyses despite differences in IDH status. Interestingly, oligodendrogliomas clustered based on patient identity and were rarely enriched for other oligodendrogliomas’ or astrocytomas’ metabolomes ( Fig. 5 ; Supplementary Fig. S4B) . Recently published work from over 200 glioma tissues demonstrated significant metabolic heterogeneity in IDH-mutant gliomas and separation of IDH-mutant from IDH-WT tumors, albeit with fewer metabolites differentiating IDH-mutant grade 4 astrocytomas from GBMs 36 . Lack of a metabolic signature of IDH-mutant gliomas beyond elevated 2-HG in our data could be due to a smaller cohort of patients lacking in low-grade astrocytomas. Indeed the clustering of grade 4 IDH-mutant astrocytomas with lower grade IDH-mutant gliomas in tissue metabolomic analyses was solely dependent on 2-HG 36 . Future studies will be needed to directly compare the extracellular versus tissue metabolome in paired patient samples. Taken together, our extracellular metabolic data suggest that despite markedly elevated D-2-HG production, IDH-mutant grade 4 astrocytomas may evolve into lesions which are otherwise almost metabolically indistinguishable from IDH-WT grade 4 astrocytomas. Having demonstrated the safety and feasibility of intraoperative microdialysis, recent expansion of our ongoing study will enable us to identify metabolic convergencies and divergencies across a larger and more diverse cohort of patients, including lower-grade astrocytomas. Further work will define the extent to which patient gender and tumor grade, in addition to clinical variables, may contribute to extracellular metabolic phenotype.

To our knowledge, our data report on a larger number of extracellular metabolites and patients than any prior study evaluating the live human glioma extracellular metabolome. Although small volumes of intraoperatively collected microdialysate enabled discovery of a large number of differentially abundant analytes via UPLC-MS/MS, analytes of potential therapeutic interest will require further validation via targeted analyses. Many of the enhancing tumor-associated metabolites confirm metabolic disruptions such as upregulation of amino acids, including proline 37 , asparagine 38 , and amino acid derivatives, such as N6-methyllysine, which have been reproducibly associated with cancer biology 39 . Accumulation of these metabolites may suggest an adaptive response to nutrient deprivation observed in gliomas 38 , 40 , 41 and other cancers 42 . Multiple carnitine family metabolites were among the top tumor-associated metabolites ( Fig. 4 ) and have been linked to energy production via fatty acid oxidation in glioma 43 , 44 .

The top tumor-associated metabolite in our data was guanidinoacetate (GAA) ( Fig. 4 ) , which accumulates in patients with GAMT deficiency, leading to impaired creatine production, seizures, and cognitive impairment 45 . To our knowledge, GAA elevation has not previously been reported in glioma, perhaps reflecting infrequent analysis of extracellular metabolites. While creatine levels were not elevated in tumor, GAA is also co-produced with ornithine, the precursor to polyamine synthesis. Polyamine synthesis via ornithine decarboxylase (ODC) is upregulated in cancers, including glioblastoma, to support catabolic demand, regulate intracellular pH in the acidic tumor core, and protect against immune surveillance 46 . It is therefore tempting to speculate that increased GAA could be indicative of increased polyamine synthesis within gliomas. If confirmed, longitudinal monitoring of GAA may augment translational studies targeting glioma polyamine synthesis. Indeed, combined blockade of ODC and polyamine transporters via DFMO and AMXT-1501, respectively, prolonged survival in preclinical models of multiple cancers, including diffuse midline gliomas 47 , 48 . We recently secured an investigational new drug (IND) approval for dual polyamine inhibition, during which we will utilize post-operative microdialysis to evaluate GAA as a pharmacodynamic biomarker of polyamine synthesis, in situ.

One limitation of microdialysis is that it does not discriminate analytes generated by the tumor from those entering the tumor microenvironment through a disrupted blood-brain barrier. One recent intraoperative study compared metabolites within the glioma’s arterial versus venous supply to determine which were consumed versus produced by the gliomas 49 . However, paired analysis of adjacent brain was not performed to discriminate tumor-from brain-associated metabolism. The combination of intra-operative microdialysis and tumor vascular sampling would help elucidate the origin of enhancing tumor-associated metabolites. Additionally, future studies could utilize focally delivered isotopes via reverse microdialysis to evaluate glioma metabolic consumption and production, in situ.

In summary, we have shown that intraoperative microdialysis for analysis of the global extracellular tumor metabolome is feasible and empowers previously unleveraged access to each patient’s diverse tumor. Our patients understand the formidable odds of their disease. We present their data as a first installment toward what we intend to be a growing collaborative, open-source repository of individualized data from live human gliomas. We (both patients and investigators) hope that the data and insights shared can help inspire novel translational paradigms. By leveraging neurosurgical access to live human disease, we hope to accelerate tangible progress within the lifetimes of individual patients.
