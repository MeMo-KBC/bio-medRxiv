ABSTRACT

By using TGIRT-seq, we identified >8,500 short full-length excised linear intron (FLEXI) RNAs in human cells. Subsets of FLEXIs accumulated in a cell-type specific manner, and ∼200 corresponded to agotrons or mirtrons or encoded snoRNAs. Analysis of CLIP-seq datasets identified potential interactions between FLEXIs and >100 different RNA-binding proteins (RBPs), 53 of which had binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXIs. In addition to proteins that function in RNA splicing, these 53 RBPs included transcription factors, chromatin remodeling proteins, and cellular growth regulators that impacted FLEXI host gene alternative splicing and/or mRNA levels in knockdown datasets. We computationally identified six groups of RBPs whose binding sites were enriched in different subsets of FLEXIs: AGO1-4 and DICER associated with agotrons and mirtrons; AATF, DKC1, NOLCI, and SMNDC1 associated with snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs; two different combinations of alternative splicing factors found in stress granules; and two novel RBP-intron combinations, one including LARP4 and PABC4, which function together in the cytoplasm to regulate ribosomal protein translation. Our results suggest a model in which proteins involved in transcriptional regulation, alternative splicing, or post-splicing secondary functions bind and stabilize cell-type specific subsets of FLEXIs that perform different biological functions and have potential utility as biomarkers.

INTRODUCTION

Most protein-coding genes in eukaryotes consist of coding regions (exons) separated by non-coding regions (introns), which are removed by RNA splicing to produce mRNAs. RNA splicing is performed by the spliceosome, a large ribonucleoprotein complex that catalyzes transesterification reactions yielding ligated exons and an excised intron lariat RNA, whose 5’ end is linked to a branch-point nucleotide near its 3’ end by a 2’,5’-phosphodiester bond ( Wilkinson et al., 2020 ). After splicing, this bond is typically hydrolyzed by a dedicated debranching enzyme (DBR1) to produce a linear intron RNA, which is rapidly degraded by cellular ribonucleases ( Chapman and Boeke, 1991 ). In a few cases, excised intron RNAs persist after excision, either as branched circular RNAs (lariats whose tails have been removed) or as unbranched linear RNAs, with some found to contribute to cellular or viral regulatory processes ( Farrell et al., 1991 ; Gardner et al., 2012 ; Kulesza and Shenk, 2006 ; Morgan et al., 2019 ; Moss and Steitz, 2013 ; Pek et al., 2015 ; Saini et al., 2019 ; Talhouarne and Gall, 2018 ; Zhang et al., 2013 ). The latter include a group of yeast introns that accumulate as unbranched linear RNAs and contribute to cell growth regulation and stress responses, possibly by sequestering spliceosomal proteins in stationary phase or other stress conditions ( Morgan et al., 2019 ; Parenteau et al., 2019 ). Other examples are mirtrons, structured excised intron RNAs that are debranched by DBR1 and processed by DICER into miRNAs ( Berezikov et al., 2007 ; Okamura et al., 2007 ; Ruby et al., 2007 ), and agotrons, structured excised linear intron RNAs that bind AGO2 and can repress target mRNAs in a miRNA-like manner ( Hansen et al., 2016 ).

Recently, we identified 44 different short (≤300 nt) full-length excised linear intron (FLEXI) RNAs in human plasma by using Thermostable Group II Intron Reverse Transcriptase sequencing (TGIRT-seq), which gives continuous end-to-end sequence reads of structured RNAs ( Yao et al., 2020 ). FLEXI RNAs were identified by peak calling as discrete blocks of continuous full- length intron reads that began and ended within 3 nt of the annotated splice sites. Subsets of plasma FLEXIs corresponded to annotated agotrons or pre-miRNAs of annotated mirtrons (denoted mirtron pre-miRNAs) ( Yao et al., 2020 ). Almost all (>95%) of the plasma FLEXIs had stable predicted secondary structures (≤-30 kcal/mol), and ∼50% contained an annotated binding site for one or more RNA-binding proteins (RBPs), which could afford protection from highly active RNases in plasma. These findings suggested that FLEXI RNAs might have utility as stable RNA biomarkers in liquid biopsies ( Yao et al., 2020 ).

Despite the precedents of agotrons and mirtrons, human excised linear intron RNAs have remained largely unexplored. Here, we used TGIRT-seq to search for FLEXIs in different human cells and tissues. We identified >8,500 different FLEXI RNAs, subsets of which accumulated in a cell-type specific manner and have potential utility as biomarkers. The identified FLEXIs included agotrons, mirtron pre-miRNAs, and snoRNA-encoding introns, as well computationally identified subsets that were enriched in binding sites for transcription factors, chromatin remodeling proteins and proteins that regulate cell growth and different steps in gene expression. Our findings suggest novel biological functions for FLEXI RNAs and provide a road map for further analyses of FLEXI-RBP interactions.

RESULTS and DISCUSSION

Identification of FLEXI RNAs in cellular RNA preparations

A search of the human genome (Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93 annotations; http://www.en-sembl.org ) identified 51,645 short introns (≤300 nt) in 12,020 different genes that could potentially give rise to FLEXI RNAs. To determine which short introns could be detected as FLEXI RNAs in human cells, we carried out TGIRT-seq of rRNA-depleted, unfragmented HEK-293T, K562, and HeLa S3 whole-cell RNAs, as well as Universal Human Reference RNA (UHRR), a commercial mixture of total RNAs from ten human cell lines ( Fig. 1 ). TGIRT-seq is well suited for the detection of excised linear intron RNAs as the high processivity and strand displacement activity of TGIRTs together with reverse transcription at elevated temperatures enable continuous full-length sequence reads of structured small non-coding RNAs (sncRNAs), which are intractable to reverse transcription by the retroviral RTs used in other RNA-seq methods ( Katibah et al., 2014 ; Qin et al., 2016 ; Shurtleff et al., 2017 ). In the TGIRT-seq datasets of unfragmented cellular RNAs obtained for this study, most of the reads corresponded to full-length mature tRNAs, Y RNAs, snoRNAs, and snRNAs ( Fig. S1 and S2). Further, because the variety of TGIRT enzyme used in this study has difficulty reading through long stretches of poly(A), reads from mature polyadenylated mRNAs comprised a low percentage of total reads (0.7-5.3%; Fig. S1 ), leading to the enrichment of reads mapping to excised intron RNAs.

To identify FLEXIs in the TGIRT-seq datasets, we compiled the coordinates of all short introns (≤300 nt) in Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93 annotations into a BED file and searched for the intersections with our data. As done previously for FLEXIs in plasma, we defined FLEXI reads as continuous intron reads that began and ended within 3 nt of the annotated splice sites ( Yao et al., 2020 ). This definition enabled us to include full-length intron reads with small numbers of extra non-templated nucleotides added by cellular processes or during TGIRT-seq library preparations, but excluded long continuous intron reads that may be biologically significant but did not satisfy the strict definition of a FLEXI at either end. The 300-nt size cutoff was based on the size distribution of FLEXIs found in human plasma ( Yao et al., 2020 ) and encompasses 85-92% of the FLEXIs detected in the different RNA preparations, including the most abundant FLEXI RNAs ( Fig. S3A ). The searches were done by using combined datasets obtained from multiple technical replicate libraries totaling 666 to 768 million mapped paired-end reads for each cellular RNA sample, with subsequent comparisons to biological replicates for four of the cell types ( Table S1 ). In addition to human cellular RNAs, we searched for FLEXIs in remapped datasets of human plasma RNAs from healthy individuals ( Yao et al., 2020 ).

By using this approach, we identified 8,144 different FLEXI RNAs originating from 3,743 different protein-coding genes, lncRNA genes, or pseudogenes (collectively denoted FLEXI host genes). The distribution of these FLEXI RNAs and their host genes between the different cellular RNA samples and plasma is shown in UpSet plots in Fig. 1A . Sixty five of the FLEXIs identified in the four cellular samples and plasma corresponded to annotated agotrons ( Hansen et al., 2016 ) and 114 corresponded to pre-miRNAs of an annotated mirtron ( Fig. 1B ), both of which were shown previously to be debranched linear intron RNAs ( Berezikov et al., 2007 ; Hansen et al., 2016 ). The proportion of FLEXI RNAs corresponding to annotated agotrons or mirtron premRNAs was higher in plasma (15.6%) than in cells (1.5-2.2%; p <1x10 -15 by Fisher’s exact test, Fig. 1B ), possibly reflecting preferential cellular export and/or greater stability of structured RNAs in plasma ( Yao et al., 2020 ). A small number of FLEXI RNAs found in cells but not plasma (43, 0.5% of the total) encoded snoRNAs, all of which were also detected as mature snoRNAs in the same samples ( Fig. 1B ).

In IGV alignments of reads mapping to a protein-coding gene, FLEXIs stand out from spliced exon and intron-exon junction reads as columns of continuous intron reads that began and ended within 3 nt of the annotated splice sites ( Fig. 2A ). The IGV alignments for 4I_JUP, an annotated agotron ( Hansen et al., 2016 ), provide an example of an intron for which a high proportion of intron reads (94%) satisfied the conservative definition of a FLEXI in HeLa S3 cells, but had 3’ truncations that resulted in lower proportions of the reads (2-3%) being counted as FLEXIs in the other three cellular RNA samples ( Fig. 2A , top left). Some FLEXIs showed a propensity for indels at runs of U residues downstream of the putative branch-point nucleotide, but no indication of impediments or misincorporations at the putative branch-point nucleotide expected for a lariat RNA ( Fig. 2A and Fig. 3B ).

A histogram of the length distribution of all reads that mapped within an intron identified as a FLEXI in any of the four cellular RNA samples showed a peak at 90-100% intron length with only a smattering corresponding to shorter fragments ( Fig. 2B ). By contrast, reads mapping within other short introns (≤300 nt) annotated in GRCh38 corresponded to heterogeneously sized RNA fragments, as expected for rapid intron turnover after splicing ( Fig. 2B ). Further, most of the FLEXI reads began and ended directly at the splice sites (87% for the 5’-splice site and 58% for the 3’-splice site), with <10% extending a short distance into a flanking exon ( Fig. 2C ). Similar FLEXI length distributions were seen in each of the four individual cellular RNA sample types, including for different subsets of FLEXIs described further below, except those containing a binding site for DICER, which showed additional discrete peaks corresponding to shorter intron fragments that may be DICER cleavage products ( Fig. S3B and C). RT-qPCR assays for 7 relatively abundant FLEXIs, including an annotated agotron (4I_JUP), confirmed that they were sensitive to digestion by Terminator (Lucigen), a commercial 5’ to 3’ exonuclease that degrades linear RNAs but not circular or lariat RNAs, which have blocked 5’ ends ( Fig. S4 ). Collectively, these findings show that TGIRT-seq can be used to identify large numbers of short full-length excised linear intron RNAs in human cells.

FLEXI RNAs have distinctive structural characteristics

Most of the 8,082 FLEXIs identified in the cellular RNA datasets have sequence characteristics of major U2-type spliceosomal introns (8,004 with GU-AG splice sites and 78 with GC-AG splice sites; Fig. 1C ), with only 36 having sequence characteristics of minor U12-type spliceosomal introns (34 with GU-AG and 2 with AU-AC splice sites; Fig. S5A ), and 23 having non-canonical splice sites (e.g., AU-AG and AU-AU; Fig. S5B ) ( Burset et al., 2000 ; Sheth et al., 2006 ). The splice-site and branch-point (BP) consensus sequences of FLEXI RNAs matched literature consensus sequences for human introns ( Burset et al., 2000 ; Gao et al., 2008 ), possibly with a higher propensity for C residues upstream of the branch-point A and 3’-splice site ( Fig. 1C for U2-type FLEXIs and Fig. S5A for U12-type FLEXIs) ( Gao et al., 2008 ). Although hydrolytic splicing, which would directly produce an excised linear intron RNA, cannot be excluded, their largely canonical branch-point sequences suggests that most if not all FLEXIs were excised as lariat RNAs and debranched after splicing, as found previously for mirtron pre-miRNAs and agotrons ( Hansen et al., 2016 ; Okamura et al., 2007 ; Ruby et al., 2007 ).

We used density plots to compare the cumulative distribution of different properties of the >8,000 FLEXIs detected in the combined TGIRT-seq datasets for human cell lines (K-562, HEK-293T, HeLa S3, and UHHR) and plasma with those of other short introns in the same datasets ( Fig. 1D ). The plots showed that FLEXI RNAs differed significantly from other short introns in being skewed toward higher GC contents and lower minimum free energies (MFEs; ΔG) for the most stable secondary structure predicted by RNAfold (p≤0.001 for both by Wilcoxon signed-rank test; Fig. 1D ), features that could contribute to their stability as FLEXIs. These differences were most pronounced for the FLEXIs detected in human plasma, which were a relatively homogeneous subset with peaks at 90-nt length, 70% GC content, and -40 kcal/mole MFE ( Fig. 1D ). By comparison, the density plots for the FLEXIs detected in cells showed similar peaks but larger shoulders extending to longer lengths, lower GC contents, and less stable predicted secondary structures (≥-25 kcal/mole; Fig. 1D ), again suggesting preferential export or greater resistance to plasma nucleases of shorter, more stably structured FLEXI RNAs.

Abundance of FLEXI RNAs

Density plots comparing the abundances (RPM) of FLEXIs in the cellular RNA samples ( Fig. 1E ) showed that they could be divided into two categories: one corresponding to low abundance FLEXIs (<0.002 RPM), which likely include transient intron RNA turnover intermediates, and the other corresponding to FLEXI RNAs that accumulate to higher abundance in different cell types (peaks between 0.002 and 0.1 RPM with a tail extending to 6.9 RPM) overlapping the lower end of the abundance distribution for snoRNAs (dashed yellow line). In HeLa S3 cells, the peak of more abundant FLEXIs was predominant, and agotrons comprised a higher proportion of more abundant FLEXIs than in the other cell types ( Fig. 1E ). The relatively abundant FLEXIs included 3,363 detected at ≥0.01 RPM in one or more of the cellular RNA samples.

To estimate copy number per cell values of FLEXI RNAs, we used sncRNAs of known cellular abundance in the TGIRT-seq datasets to produce a linear regression model for the relationship between sequence-bias corrected log 10 RPM values and log 10 transformed sncRNA copy number per cell values from the literature ( Fig. S6A ; Table S2 ). By using this method, we estimated that moderately abundant FLEXIs (0.01-0.99 RPM) were present at 15 to 239 copies per cell, and the most abundant FLEXIs (≥1 RPM) were present at 363 to 1,863 copies per cell (see linear regression plots in Fig. S6A ). Based on the linear regression model, FLEXIs previously annotated as agotrons were present at 1 to 1,335 copies per cell; mirtron pre-miRNA FLEXIs at 1 to 225 copies per cell; and snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs at 1 to 265 copies per cell, where 1 is the minimum copy number cell value that could be estimated from the linear regression. Copy per cell values determined by droplet digital PCR (ddPCR) for a sampling of FLEXIs were generally of the same order of magnitude as the TGIRT-seq quantitation, but varied with the choice of sncRNA standard, with the most abundant FLEXIs estimated to be present at 3,000 to 4,000 copies per cell similar to that of U7 RNA when the latter was used as a standard ( Fig. S6B ).

Abundant FLEXI RNAs have cell-type specific expression patterns

Scatter plots of FLEXIs detected in pairwise comparisons of different cell lines identified numerous relatively abundant FLEXI RNAs (≥0.01 RPM) that were reproducibly detected and differentially expressed between the two compared cell types ( Fig. 3A and Fig. S7 ). Evolutionary conservation of FLEXIs

PhastCons scores provide a measure of evolutionary conservation, with higher phastCons scores indicating a higher probability that a sequence is conserved and functionally important across a range of organisms ( Siepel et al., 2005 ). Density plots of phastCons scores calculated across 27 primates including humans plus mouse, dog, and armadillo showed that most FLEXIs, including those corresponding to mirtron pre-miRNAs or agotrons, were poorly conserved (peaks at 0.06-0.09 compared to 0.02 for other annotated short introns in GRCh38), but with tails extending to higher phastCons scores. FLEXIs encoding snoRNAs had higher phastCons scores (four at ≥0.5) than did other FLEXIs, likely indicating conservation of the snoRNA component ( Fig. 1F , yellow line). The relatively low phastCons scores of most FLEXIs could reflect recent acquisition and/or rapid sequence divergence in the human lineage.

Although most FLEXI RNAs were poorly conserved, 2% (158) of the FLEXIs that were not annotated as mirtrons, agotrons or encoding snoRNAs had phastCons scores ≥0.75, possibly reflecting an evolutionarily conserved sequence-dependent function. Compared to those with lower phastCons scores, these more highly conserved FLEXIs were enriched in introns that were alternatively spliced (69% compared to 35%), shared a 5’- or 3’-splice site with a longer intron (44% compared to 18%), corresponded to a retained intron (47% compared to 25%), or contained an in-frame protein-coding sequence that would be expressed if all or part of the intron was retained within a mRNA (20% compared to 7.8%; Fig. 3C ). The proportion of FLEXIs with these characteristics was even higher for the 44 most highly conserved FLEXIs (phastCons scores ≥0.99; Fig. 3C ). A FLEXI in the human EIF1 gene with phastCons score = 1.00 resulted from acquisition of a 3’-splice site in its highly conserved 3’ UTR leading to expression of a novel human-specific EIF1 isoform (chr17:41,690,818-41,690,902; Supplemental File) ( Kim et al., 2020 ). These findings suggest that in most if not all cases the sequence conservation of FLEXIs with high phastCons scores reflects evolutionary constraints on different protein isoforms produced by alternative splicing.

FLEXIs contain binding sites for >100 different RBPs with diverse cellular functions

To explore possible biological functions of FLEXIs, we searched for RBPs that have binding sites in FLEXI RNAs in CLIP-seq datasets (ENCODE high confidence eCLIP datasets for K562 and HepG2 cells ( Van Nostrand et al., 2016 ) and AGO1-4 and DICER PAR-CLIP datasets for HEK-293 cells ( Hafner et al., 2010 ; Rybak-Wolf et al., 2014 )). More than half of the detected FLEXIs (4,505; 55%) contained a CLIP-seq-identified binding site for one or more of 126 different RBPs, with 53 RBPs (51 in the eCLIP datasets plus DICER and AGO1-4 in the PAR-CLIP datasets) having binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXI RNAs ( Fig. 4A , Fig. S9A ). These 53 RBPs included spliceosomal proteins and other proteins that function in RNA splicing; DICER, AGO1-4 and other proteins that have miRNA-related functions; and a surprising number of proteins that function in cellular regulatory processes, including transcriptional regulation, chromatin assembly and disassembly, cellular growth regulation, and stress responses ( Fig. 4A and Table S3 ). Although cross-linking does not distinguish whether the RBP was bound to the intron before or after splicing, examination of sequence reads in the ENCODE eCLIP datasets showed that all of the RBPs that have a binding site in ≥30 different FLEXIs were cross-linked to ≥95% full-length intron RNAs corresponding to identified FLEXIs, with 32 showing a ≥2-fold enrichment of such reads compared to the control antibody datasets ( Fig. S10 ). This finding indicates that whether or not the RBP bound to the intron before or after splicing, some fraction of each RBP remained bound to an excised intron RNA and may have protected it from RNase I digestion used for processing the cross-linked RNAs for sequencing in the ENCODE datasets.

RBPs that bind FLEXIs also bind other short and long introns

All 53 proteins in the ENCODE eCLIP and PAR-CLIP datasets that have cross-link identified binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXIs also bind thousands of other short and long introns ( Fig. S9B and C). Scatter plots showed that RBPs whose binding sites were significantly over-represented in FLEXIs compared to long introns (significant enrichment defined as ≥1% of total RBPbinding sites; p≤0.05, Fisher’s exact test) included spliceosomal proteins, as well as AGO1-4, DICER, DDX24, GRWD1 (a histone-binding protein that functions in ribosome assembly and regulates chromatin dynamics), RBM22 (a spliceosomal component that may play a role in regulating cell division), UCHL5 (ubiquitin carboxy-terminal hydrolase), and ZNF622 (a putative transcriptional activator that is a positive regulator of apoptosis; Fig. 4B ), with AGO1-4, DICER, and RBM22 also significantly over-represented in FLEXIs compared to other short introns ( Fig. 4C ). The scatter plots also identified numerous proteins whose binding sites were over-represented in other short or long introns compared to FLEXIs ( Fig. 4B and C ). The over-representation of spliceosomal protein-binding sites in FLEXI RNAs may reflect that most FLEXIs dissociate more slowly from spliceosomes than do long introns, providing greater opportunity for cross-linking. If so, the abundance of FLEXIs as a whole might be sufficiently high to affect intracellular levels of spliceosomal proteins in response to stimuli that globally affect FLEXI RNA turnover, as found for the collective of yeast linear introns that accumulate under stress conditions ( Morgan et al., 2019 ).

Mapping of RBP-binding sites in intron RNAs

To gain insight into how RBP binding might affect the production and function of FLEXI RNAs, we collected CLIP-seq reads containing intron sequences for the 53 RBPs that have a binding site in ≥30 FLEXIs in the ENCODE eCLIP and AGO1-4 and DICER PAR-CLIP datasets and mapped their binding sites in FLEXIs and in other categories of introns ( Fig. 5C and Fig. S13 , results summarized in Fig. 4A ). We found that 37 of these proteins bind at or near the 5’- and 3’- splice sites (SS), while 16 have binding sites more uniformly distributed throughout the body of the intron (I). For those proteins that bind at or near the splice sites, the midpoint of the annotated binding site peak in the FLEXI RNA was either at the splice site or immediately adjacent to it within the intron (SS-I) or flanking exon (SS-E). Notably, the RBPs whose binding sites were at or near the 5’- and 3’-splice sites included not only splicing factors, but also many proteins whose primary function is not RNA splicing, including AGO1-4, BCLAF1 (BCL2-2-associated transcription factor), DDX3X, DDX24, DDX55, FXR2, G3BP1, GEMIN5, GPKOW, GRWD1, IGF2BP, LARP4, LIN28B, LSM11, METAP2, NCBP2, PABPC4, PABPN1, PUM1, RPS3, SND1, SRSF1, SUB1 (Transcriptional Regulator), UCHL5, YBX3, ZNF622, and ZNF800). The RBPs that have binding sites more uniformly distributed throughout the FLEXI included AATF (Apoptosis Antagonizing Transcription Factor), DICER, DKC1 (Dyskerin), EFTUD2, KHSRP, NOLC1, PCBP1, PCBP2, PRPF4, RBFOX2, RBM5, RBM15, RBM22, SMNDC1, TIA1, and XRN2, a nuclear 5’ to 3’ exonuclease that degrades excised linear intron RNAs ( Fig. 5C and Fig. S13 ). In some cases, RBPs whose binding sites were localized near the 5’- and 3’-splice sites in long introns had binding sites more centrally localized in FLEXIs (e.g., RBM5; Fig. 5C ; RBM15, TIA1, TIAL1; Fig. S13 ). The RBP-binding site locations suggest possible mechanisms by which individual RBPs may affect the production and/or stability of FLEXI RNAs.

Identification of RBP-binding sites enriched in agotrons, mirtrons, snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs, and evolutionarily conserved FLEXIs

Although FLEXIs as a whole are enriched in binding sites for spliceosomal proteins ( Fig. 4B ), it remained possible that subsets of FLEXI RNAs might be enriched in binding sites for RBPs that perform specialized biological functions. Such FLEXIs seemed likely to include agotrons and mirtrons, which bind AGO1-4 and DICER, proteins that recognize branch-point occluding secondary structures whose formation may require full or partial dissociation of the spliceosome ( Berezikov et al., 2007 ; Hansen et al., 2016 ). Consistent with this prediction, scatter plots confirmed that FLEXIs annotated as agotrons (n=61) or mirtrons (n=110) in the cellular RNA samples were significantly enriched in binding sites for AGO1-4 and DICER relative to those for spliceosomal or other proteins (significant enrichment defined as ≥2% of total RBP-binding sites; p ≤0.05 by Fisher’s exact test; Fig. 6A and B , left panel). Density plots showed that the cellular FLEXI RNAs annotated as agotrons were a smaller, more homogeneously sized GC-rich subset of FLEXIs than were pre-miRNAs of annotated mirtrons ( Fig. 6A and B , right panel).

The latter protein was found previously to bind both DKC1 transcripts and snoRNAs, potentially linking the ribosome biosynthesis function of snoRNAs to the regulation of cell proliferation, apoptosis, and p53 activity ( Kaiser et al., 2019 ) (see legend Fig. 6C for differences in RBP-binding sites in FLEXIs encoding H/ACA-box and CD-box snoRNAs).

Finally, the 158 highly conserved FLEXIs (phastCons scores ≥ 0.75), which included a high proportion of alternatively spliced introns (see above), were significantly enriched in binding sites for splicing factor U2AF2 and splicing regulator TIAL1 as well as the transcriptional regulators BCLAF1 and ZNF622, potentially linking alternative splicing of these introns to transcriptional and other cell regulatory pathways ( Fig. 6D ; Table S3 ), a role previously suggested for BCLAF1 ( Savage et al., 2014 ; Vohhodina et al., 2017 ). Collectively, the above findings show that specific subsets of FLEXI RNAs are enriched in binding sites for RBPs that preform specialized biological functions relative to those for spliceosomal proteins and led us to wonder whether such enrichment might be a distinguishing feature of RBPs that bind or remain bound to excised intron RNAs after complete or partial dissociation from the spliceosome.

Computational identification of RBPs whose binding sites are co-enriched in different subsets of FLEXI RNAs

Encouraged by the above findings, we attempted to mathematically identify other RBPs whose binding sites were co-enriched in different subsets of FLEXIs RNAs without a priori assumptions about the function of the protein or bound FLEXIs. To do so, we first extracted subsets of FLEXIs with a CLIP-seq identified binding site for 47 of the 53 RBPs that bind ≥30 different FLEXIs in the combined CLIP-seq datasets, excluding six core spliceosomal proteins (AQR, BUD13, EFTUD2, PPIG, PRPF8, and SF3B4), whose abundant binding sites would otherwise complicate the analysis. We then identified which of the 126 RBPs that bind FLEXIs were coenriched in each of the 47 subsets by comparing the frequencies of different RBP-binding sites in each subset to those in all FLEXs (significant enrichment defined as abundance ≥2%, adjusted p- value ≤0.05). RBPs whose binding sites were significantly over- or under-represented in the tested subset and those that did not meet these criteria were key coded, and hierarchal clustering was performed using the Gower’s distance between the 47 subsets ( Gower, 1971 ). The results were displayed as a dendrogram heat map for FLEXIs in each of the four cellular RNA datasets (HEK-293T, K-562, HeLa S3, and UHHR; Fig. S14 ).

FLEXI RNAs associated with RBP clusters originate from host genes with related biological functions

To determine if the host genes encoding FLEXIs in the six identified clusters might be functionally related, we analyzed gene ontology (GO) terms of host genes bound by the same RBPs using DAVID bioinformatic tools and displayed the results as a heat map for each of the RBPs ( Fig. 7B ). Overall, the heat map for GO terms related to host gene function was remarkably congruent with the identified RBP clusters. The host genes encoding FLEXIs bound by AGO1-4 and DICER (Cluster VI) encode proteins involved in translation and focal adhesion as well as structural components of the ribosome and proteins found in nuclear speckles, while those encoding FLEXIs in cluster II, III, and IV encode proteins involved in RNA splicing, gene expression and mRNA processing, with the host genes for FLEXIs bound by some proteins cluster II also involved in cell-cell adhesion. The host genes encoding FLEXIs bound by the snoRNA-related proteins in cluster V have functions related to ribosomes, translation, and rRNA processing, with the host genes for FLEXIs bound SMNDC1 enriched in additional GO terms. Notably, the GO terms for the host genes encoding FLEXIs in clusters I, which have binding sites LARP4 and PABPC4, which function together in regulating the stability and translation of ribosomal protein mRNAs, largely overlap those for the host genes for the snoRNA-related FLEXIs in cluster V and were similarly enriched in genes encoding ribosomal proteins (12% and 10.6% compared to 1.4% in all FLEXIs; p<0.001), suggesting that cluster I FLEXIs may function in a parallel pathway for regulating ribosome biogenesis. Collectively, these findings indicate that the host genes encoding FLEXI RNAs that bind the same subsets of RBPs are functionally related and could be coordinately regulated to produce different subsets FLEXI RNPs.

FLEXI as potential cancer biomarkers

The cell type-specific expression patterns of FLEXI RNAs suggested that they might be useful as RNA biomarkers. As an initial test of this possibility, we compared FLEXI RNAs in matched tumor and neighboring healthy tissue from two breast cancer patients (patients A and B; PR + , ER + , HER2 - and PR unknown, ER - , HER2 - , respectively) and two breast cancer cell lines (MDA-MB- 231 and MCF7). UpSet plots for the relatively abundant FLEXIs (≥0.01 RPM) showed hundreds of differences in FLEXI RNAs and FLEXI host genes between the cancer and healthy samples ( Fig. 8A ). Further, scatter plots comparing the FLEXI RNAs detected in the matched healthy and tumor samples from patients A and B identified multiple candidate FLEXI RNA biomarkers (5 and 43 in patients A and B, respectively) that were detected at ≥0.01 RPM in all replicate libraries from the cancer patient tissue, but not detected in the matched healthy tissue (red dots and protein names in red in Fig. 8B ). As for the previous comparisons between different cellular RNA samples ( Fig. 3A ), the scatter plots showed a wider spectrum of differences than did those for mRNAs from the same host genes ( Fig. 8B ). Notably, FLEXI RNAs were more abundant in the highly metastatic MDA-MB-231 cells than in MCF-7 cells, with density plot profiles suggesting upregulation of snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs and agotrons in MDA-MB-231 ( Fig. S17 ).

Summary, Perspectives, and Limitations

Here, we used TGIRT-seq of rRNA-depleted, unfragmented RNA preparations to identify thousands of FLEXI RNAs in human cells and tissues. In total, including the initial cellular and plasma RNA samples and subsequent cancer samples, we identified 8,687 different FLEXIs expressed from 3,923 host genes, representing ∼17% of the 51,645 short introns (≤300 nt) annotated in Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93 annotations. That FLEXIs are linear RNAs was inferred from the characteristics of TGIRT-seq reads ( Fig. 2 ) and confirmed by digestion with a 5’ to 3’ exonuclease ( Fig. S4 ). Most FLEXIs have high GC content and stable predicted secondary structures, which may contribute to their stability as FLEXIs ( Fig. 1D ). In addition to low abundance FLEXIs, which likely include transient intron RNA turnover intermediates, we identified subsets of FLEXIs in each cell type that accumulated to relatively high abundance, including many with abundances of hundreds to thousands of copies per cell ( Fig. 1E and Fig. S6 ). These relatively abundant FLEXIs had reproducible cell-type specific expression patterns ( Fig. 3 , Fig. S7 , and Fig. S8 ), suggesting that FLEXIs may have utility as RNA biomarkers in cells as well as plasma.

By searching CLIP-seq datasets, we identified 126 different RBPs that have annotated binding sites in FLEXI RNAs, including 53 RBPs that have binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXIs on which we focused further analysis ( Fig. 4A ). These 53 RPBs included spliceosomal proteins and other proteins that function in RNA splicing, AGO1-4 and DICER and other proteins that have a miRNA-related functions, and a surprising number of proteins that function in cell regulatory processes, including transcription factors (AATF, BCLAF1, and SUB1, a chromatin remodeling protein (GRWD1), proteins that function in translational regulation (LARP4, METAP2, PABC4), and cellular growth regulators (DDX3 and IGF2B1; Fig. 4A ; Table S3 ). Although CLIP-seq does not distinguish whether an RBP bound to an intron before or after splicing, all of the RBPs identified as having binding sites in ≥30 FLEXI RNAs in the ENCODE eCLIP datasets were associated with ≥95% full-length reads corresponding to identified FLEXIs, despite the use of RNase I in processing the cross-linked RNAs for sequencing ( Fig. 4 and Fig. S10 ). The ability RBPs to protect short excised intron RNAs from nucleases that function in intron turnover presumably contributes to their accumulation as FLEXIs.

Addressing the biological significance of FLEXI-RBP interactions by analysis of ENCODE knockdown datasets, we found that knockdown of 34 of the RBPs that have binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXIs resulted in significant changes in alternative splicing of bound FLEXIs and/or

mRNA levels of FLEXIs that contained a binding site for that RBP relative to control groups ( Fig. 4A , Fig. 5A and B , Fig. S11 , and Fig. S12 ). Surprisingly 17 of the 27 RBPs whose knockdown impacted alternative splicing of bound FLEXIs had no previously known RNA splicing- related function, including the cell regulatory proteins AATF, RPS3, SUB1, GRWD1, IGF2B, LARP4, PABC4, and XRN2 a nuclear 5’ to 3’ exonuclease that degrades linear RNAs, (see Results for complete list). The involvement of transcription, translation, and cell growth regulatory proteins in RNA splicing provides a means of linking splicing to other cellular regulatory pathways, a role suggested previously for BCLAF1 ( Savage et al., 2014 ; Vohhodina et al., 2017 ) and for binding of AATF to snoRNAs ( Kaiser et al., 2019 ).

By mapping RBP-binding sites in CLIP-seq datasets, we found that 37 of the 53 RBPs that bind ≥30 FLEXI RNAs bind at or near the 5’- and 3’-splice sites, while 16 have binding sites more uniformly distributed throughout the body of the intron ( Fig. 5C and Fig. S13 ). The binding of RBPs to the 5’- and 3’-splice sites in a pre-mRNA could be facilitated for FLEXIs that fold into a stable secondary structure that brings the 5’- and 3’-splice sites into proximity, as suggested for the binding of the spliceosome to the 5’- and 3’-spice sites of human short introns ( Abebrese et al., 2017 ). Alternatively, some RBPs may bind to the exposed 5’ and 3’ ends of the FLEXI RNA after complete or partial spliceosome dissociation. In either case, RBPs bound simultaneously to the 5’- and 3’-ends of an excised intron would effectively circularize the intron RNA, protecting it from exonucleases that function in intron RNA turnover and potentially contributing to its accumulation as a FLEXI.

Compared to other short and long introns, FLEXIs as a whole were enriched in binding sites for core spliceosomal proteins ( Fig. 4B and C ), possibly reflecting that most FLEXIs dissociate more slowly from the spliceosome than other introns, providing greater opportunity for crosslinking. However, we also found that subsets of FLEXIs with known or hypothesized biological functions were enriched in binding sites for RBPs related to those functions, possibly reflecting that these RBPs remained bound to the excised intron RNA after dissociation from the spliceosome. These subsets of FLEXIs included agotrons and mirtrons, which were enriched in binding sites for AGO1-4 and DICER ( Fig. 6A and B ); snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs, which were enriched in proteins with snoRNA-related functions ( Fig. 6C ); and evolutionarily conserved FLEXIs, which were enriched in binding sites for transcription factors and splicing regulators that may control the production of different protein isoforms from FLEXI host genes ( Fig. 6D ).

Building on these findings, we used a computational method with no a priori assumptions about RBP or FLEXI function to identify six clusters of proteins whose binding sites were coenriched relative to those for spliceosomal and other proteins in different subsets of FLEXI RNAs ( Fig. 7 and Fig. S14 ). This computational method recapitulated the findings that agotrons and mirtrons are enriched in binding sites for AGO1-4 and DICER and that snoRNA-encoding FLEXIs are enriched in binding sites for snoRNA related proteins, but also identified many additional FLEXIs bound by these proteins that were not previously annotated as agotrons, mirtrons, or snoRNA-encoding introns. The computational method also identified two sets of splicing factors and regulators that were co-enriched in different subsets of FLEXIs, and two groups of RBPs that may relate to novel functions of FLEXI RNAs ( Fig. 7A ). Particularly suggestive of the latter was cluster I, which included two proteins, LARP4 and PABC4, which were known to function together to regulate the stability and translation of ribosomal protein mRNAs ( Mattijssen et al., 2017 ), along with additional proteins that have translation-related functions (NCBP2, METAP2, and RPS3), or that could link these functions to transcriptional regulation of other cell regulatory pathways (SUB1, RPS3, DDX3X, and DDX55). Further, the host genes encoding the FLEXIs in cluster I have GO terms similar to those for the snoRNA-related FLEXIs in cluster V ( Fig. 7B ) suggesting that the FLEXIs and RBPs of cluster I may function similarly to snoRNAs in a feedback loop regulating ribosome assembly or function ( Fig. 7B ). Along the same lines, cluster II included three proteins (UCHL5, ZNF622, and TRA2A) that were distinguished from other proteins associated with FLEXI/RBP clusters as being localized to ER, cytoskeleton, and cell cortex, possibly reflecting a cytoskeleton or membrane-associated function, and clusters III and IV consisted of five splicing factors or regulators, four of which localize to stress granules (TIA1, TIAL1, U2AF1, and KHSRP) ( Nunes et al., 2019 ), possibly a post-splicing destination for the bound FLEXIs ( Fig. 7A ). Collectively, these findings suggest that some FLEXIs may function as scaffold to co-localize or co-regulate multiple proteins with related biological functions.

Our computational identification of intron-RBP associations is undoubtedly incomplete, as it depends upon assigning a relatively arbitrary significance criterion for association of an RBP with the cluster and would miss proteins that are not efficiently cross-linked to FLEXI RNAs. Minimally, however, our findings demonstrate that different subsets of RBPs bind specifically to distinct subsets of FLEXIs and set the stage for more detailed analysis of these RBP-intron associations.

Finally, the relatively abundant FLEXIs that have cell-type specific expression patterns may have utility as cellular RNA biomarkers. In addition to being linked to the transcription of thousands of protein-coding and lncRNA genes, FLEXI RNA levels also reflect differences in alternative splicing and intron RNA stability, providing higher resolution of cellular differences than mRNAs transcribed from the same gene. Because many FLEXIs are predicted to fold into stable secondary structures, their initial identification may be best done by TGIRT-seq, which yields full-length, end-to-end sequence reads of structured RNAs, possibly utilizing size-selected small RNA preparations in which FLEXIs are likely to be enriched. Once identified, the validation of candidate FLEXI biomarkers and their routine monitoring would best be done by methods such as RT-qPCR, microarrays, other hybridization-based assays, or targeted RNA-seq that can give rapid quantitative read outs in targeted RNA panels for different diseases.

Methods

DNA and RNA oligonucleotides

DNA and RNA oligonucleotides were purchased from Integrated DNA Technologies (IDT) in RNase-free, HPLC-purified form. The DNA and RNA oligonucleotides used for TGIRT-seq are shown in Table S4 . R2R DNA oligonucleotides with 3’ A, C, G, and T residues were handmixed in equimolar amounts prior to annealing to the R2 RNA oligonucleotide to generate RNA/DNA heteroduplexes with a 1-nt 3’-DNA overhang for RNA-seq adapter addition by TGIRT template switching.

The oligonucleotides used Terminator exonuclease and ddPCR experiments are listed in Table S5 and include reverse transcriptase primers, PCR primers, and the 3I_RAN FLEXI synthetic oligonucleotide controls with and without a 5’ phosphate. The sequence specificity of all primer sets ( Table S5 ) was confirmed by gel purifying a representative subset of qPCR amplicons and either directly Sanger sequencing or by TOPO-TA cloning (ThermoFisher) followed by Sanger sequencing. A circular form of the 3I_RAN FLEXI synthetic oligonucleotide control was generated by incubating the 5’phosphorylated-3I_RAN FLEXI oligonucleotide (500 ng) with T4 RNA Ligase I (10 U; New England Biolabs) for 2 h at 25 °C and 2 min at 95 °C. The circularized oligonucleotide was cleaned up with an RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (Zymo Research), using 8 sample volumes of ethanol added during purification (denoted 8X ethanol method) to maximize the recovery of small RNAs concurrently with the longer RNAs.

RNA preparations

Universal Human Reference RNA (UHRR) was purchased from Agilent (product name: Quantitative PCR Human Reference Total RNA), and HeLa S3 and MCF-7 RNAs were purchased from Thermo Fisher. RNAs from matched frozen healthy/tumor tissues of breast cancer patients were purchased from Origene (500 ng; Patient A: PR + , ER + , HER2 - , CR562524/CR543839; Patient B: PR unknown, ER - , HER2 - , CR560540/CR532030).

K-562, HEK-293T/17, and MDA-MB-231 RNAs were isolated from cultured cells by using a mirVana miRNA Isolation Kit (Thermo Fisher). K-562 cells (ATCC CTL-243) were maintained in Iscove’s Modified Dulbecco’s Medium (IMDM) + 4 mM L-glutamine and 25 mM HEPES; Thermo Fisher) supplemented with 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS; Gemini Bio-Products), and approximately 2 x 10 6 cells were used for RNA extraction. HEK-293T/17 cells (ATCC CRL-11268) were maintained in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) + 4.5 g/L D-glucose, 4 mM L-glutamine, and 1 mM sodium pyruvate, supplemented with 10% FBS, and approximately 4 x 10 6 cells were used for RNA extraction. MDA-MB-231 cells (ATCC HTB-26) were maintained in DMEM + 4.5 g/L D-glucose and 4 mM L-glutamine (Thermo Fisher) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1X PSQ (Penicillin, Streptomycin, and Glutamine: Thermo Fisher), and approximately 4 x 10 6 cells were used for RNA extraction. All cells were maintained at 37 °C in a humidified 5% CO 2 atmosphere.

For RNA isolation, cells were harvested by centrifugation (after trypsinization for HEK- 293T/17 and MDA-MB-231 cells) at 300 x g for 10 min at 4 °C and washed twice by centrifugation with cold Dulbecco’s Phosphate Buffered Saline (Thermo Fisher). The indicated number of cells (see above) was then resuspended in 600 µL of mirVana Lysis Buffer and RNA was isolated according to the kit manufacturer’s protocol with elution in a final volume of 100 µL.

Terminator exonuclease assay

Unfragmented HEK-293T and UHHR total RNAs (500 ng) or synthetic 3I_RAN FLEXI oligonucleotide controls (100 ng; oligonucleotide with or without a 5’phosphate or circularized oligonucleotide) were treated with 1 U Terminator exonuclease (Lucigen) or mock treated in the manufacturer’s Buffer A for 1 h at 37 °C followed by 1 h at 30 °C. The terminator reactions were cleaned up with the RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (8X ethanol method; Zymo Research). The manufacturer’s instructions note that the standard buffer (Buffer A) is the preferred reaction buffer for maximal exonuclease activity, but that non-5’-monophosphate-specific activity, including activity with RNAs having a 5’ hydroxyl activity, might also result. Reverse transcription was performed by using a gene specific primer (1 μM final concentration) located near the 3’ end of the FLEXI intron with 100 U Maxima H reverse transcriptase (ThermoFisher) in the manufacturer’s buffer and incubated at 25 °C for 10 min, 50 °C for 30 min, 65 °C for 30 min and 85 °C for 5 min. qPCR reactions were carried out with 1 μL cDNA and PowerUp SYBR Green master mix (Life Technologies) using a ViiA 7 Real Time PCR system (ThermoFisher). Delta CT values were calculated to obtain a fold change of RNA levels in the treated compared to the mock treated sample. The primer sequences and 3I_RAN FLEXI synthetic oligonucleotide control sequences are shown in Table S5 .

Droplet digital PCR

Unfragmented HeLa S3, UHRR or HEK-293T RNAs (5 μg) were DNased with 2 U TURBO DNase (Invitrogen) and purified using an RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (Zymo Research) following the manufacturer’s small-size selection method, to separate RNAs ≤200 nt. The input of the size-selected RNAs for reverse transcription reactions were scaled to have a similar SNORD14B control amplification when compared with total RNA inputs (HEK-293T and HeLa S3, 5 ng; UHHR, 1 ng). The RNAs were reverse transcribed by using 1 μM final concentration of a gene specific primer ( Table S5 ) located at the 3’ end of the FLEXI intron with 100 U Maxima H Reverse Transcriptase (ThermoFisher) in the manufacturer’s buffer and incubated at 25 °C for 10 min, 50 °C for 30 min, 65 °C for 30 min and 85 °C for 5 min. Matched control samples containing the small size-selected RNA, but omitting the RT (RT-) were performed for every reaction. Droplet digital PCR (ddPCR) was performed on 1 μL cDNA using QX200 ddPCR EvaGreen Supermix (Biorad) according to manufacturer’s protocol with the QX200 Droplet Digital PCR system (BioRad). Primer sets ( Table S5 ) were designed to encompass either the FLEXI intron sequence or to span the upstream 5’exon-FLEXI junction in order to quantify the FLEXI RNA compared to any precursor or mature mRNA with retained FLEXI introns. The same genespecific-generated cDNA was used as the PCR template for quantifying both the FLEXI and 5’ exon-FLEXI junctions. Due to the short size of the FLEXI sequences, primer design was constrained to the best available primer location that maximized length and sequence specificity. As a result of the primer constraints, some primers amplified a smaller region of the FLEXI sequence than the TGIRT-seq criteria (i.e., 5’ and 3’ ends within 3 nt of the annotated splice sites), which could contribute to differences between ddPCR and TGIRT-seq quantification. 5’-exon- FLEXI junction primer pairs used a different reverse primer than the FLEXI, that was inset closer to the 5’exon-FLEXI junction, so that the size of the amplicon would be more similar to the size of the compared FLEXI amplicon. As a consequence of these constraints, some primer pairs amplified background non-specific products in addition to verified products. For each reaction replicate, any RT- signal was subtracted, and three background-subtracted reaction replicates were averaged for each primer pair. The majority of the 5’exon-FLEXI junction reactions either gave no product or amplified non-specific products. However, a few 5’exon-FLEXI junction reactions produced validated products sequenced by direct Sanger sequencing and/or TOPOTA cloning and Sanger sequencing of gel extracted, matched qPCR amplicons (HEK-293T, RPS2; HeLa S3, ACTB; UHRR, POLG and ACTB). These sequence-validated 5’exon-FLEXI junction ddPCR signals were subtracted from the FLEXI ddPCR signals to obtain the final ddPCR estimate for abundance of the FLEXI. FLEXI copies per reaction were then normalized to the controls performed in parallel: U7 (chosen for similar abundance to FLEXIs), SNORD14B ( Dodd et al., 2013 ), and SNORD44 ( Sharbati et al., 2012 ). Finally, the normalized FLEXI copies per reaction were converted to copy number per cell estimates by multiplying by respective control values generated by the linear regression model without or with TGIRT-seq bias correction.

TGIRT-seq

To remove residual DNA, UHRR and HeLa S3 RNAs (1 μg) and patients A and B healthy and cancer tissue RNAs (500 ng) were incubated with 20 U exonuclease I (Lucigen) and 2 U Baseline-ZERO DNase (Lucigen) in Baseline-ZERO DNase Buffer for 30 min at 37 °C, and K562, MDA-MB-231 and HEK-293T cell RNAs (5 μg) were incubated with 2 U TURBO DNase for 30 min at 37 °C (ThermoFisher). After DNA digestion, RNA was cleaned up with an RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (8X ethanol method; Zymo Research). The eluted RNAs were ribodepleted by using the rRNA removal section of a TruSeq Stranded Total RNA Library Prep Human/Mouse/Rat kit (Illumina), with the supernatant from the magnetic-bead separation cleaned-up by using a Zymo RNA Clean & Concentrator kit with 8X ethanol. After checking RNA concentration and length by using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer with a 6000 RNA Pico chip, RNAs were aliquoted into ∼20 ng portions and stored at -80 °C until use.

For the preparation of samples containing chemically fragmented long RNAs, RNA preparations were treated with exonuclease I and Baseline-Zero DNase to remove residual DNA and ribodepleted, as described above. The supernatant from the magnetic-bead separation after ribodepletion was then cleaned-up with a Zymo RNA Clean & Concentrator kit using the manufacturer’s two-fraction protocol, which separates RNAs into long and short RNA fractions (200- nt cut-off). The long RNAs were then fragmented to 70-100 nt by using an NEBNext Magnesium RNA Fragmentation Module (94 °C for 7 min; New England Biolabs). After clean-up by using a Zymo RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (8X ethanol protocol), the fragmented long RNAs were combined with the unfragmented short RNAs and treated with T4 polynucleotide kinase (Epicentre) to remove 3’ phosphates ( Xu et al., 2019 ), followed by clean-up using a Zymo RNA Clean & Concentrator kit (8X ethanol protocol). After confirming the RNA fragment size range and RNA concentration by using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer with a 6000 RNA Pico chip, the RNA was aliquoted into 4 ng portions for storage in -80 °C.

TGIRT-seq libraries were prepared as described ( Xu et al., 2019 ) using 20-50 ng of ribodepleted unfragmented cellular RNA or 4-10 ng of ribodepleted chemically fragmented cellular RNA. Template-switching and reverse transcription reactions were done with 1 μM TGIRT-III (InGex) and 100 nM pre-annealed R2 RNA/R2R DNA starter duplex in 20 μl of reaction medium containing 450 mM NaCl, 5 mM MgCl 2 , 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5 and 5 mM DTT. Reactions were set up with all components except dNTPs, pre-incubated for 30 min at room temperature, a step that increases the efficiency of RNA-seq adapter addition by TGIRT template switching, and initiated by adding dNTPs (final concentrations 1 mM each of dATP, dCTP, dGTP, and dTTP). The reactions were incubated for 15 min at 60 °C and then terminated by adding 1 μl 5 M NaOH to degrade RNA and heating at 95 °C for 5 min followed by neutralization with 1 μl 5 M HCl and one round of MinElute column clean-up (Qiagen). The R1R DNA adapter was adenylated by using a 5’ DNA Adenylation kit (New England Biolabs) and then ligated to the 3’ end of the cDNA by using thermostable 5’ App DNA/RNA Ligase (New England Biolabs) for 2 h at 65 °C. The ligated products were purified by using a MinElute Reaction Cleanup Kit and amplified by PCR with Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific): denaturation at 98 °C for 5 sec followed by 12 cycles of 98 °C 5 sec, 60 °C 10 sec, 72 °C 15 sec and then held at 4 °C until removed. The PCR products were cleaned up by using Agencourt AMPure XP beads (1.4X volume; Beckman Coulter) and sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq 500 to obtain 2 x 75 nt paired-end reads or on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 to obtain 2 x 150 nt paired- end reads at the Genome Sequence and Analysis Facility of the University of Texas at Austin.

TGIRT-seq datasets of commercial human plasma pooled from multiple healthy individuals were obtained in a previous study ( Yao et al., 2020 ) and deposited in the National Center for Biotechnology Information Sequence Read Archive under accession number PRJNA640428.

TGIRT-seq datasets for biological replicates of unfragmented cellular RNAs from HEK-293T were previously deposited in the National Center for Biotechnology Information Sequence Read Archive under accession number PRJNA389469 (samples WC and YC). TGIRT-seq datasets for biological replicates of unfragmented cellular RNAs from K-562, MDA-MB-231 and UHRR were previously deposited in the National Center for Biotechnology Information Sequence Read Archive under accession number PRJNA722757 (K-562 cells, samples UFC1-7; MDA-MB-231, samples MDA1-5; and UHRR, samples UHRR0-9).

Sequencing data processing

Illumina TruSeq adapters and PCR primer sequences were trimmed from the reads with Cutadapt v2.8 (sequencing quality score cut-off at 20; p-value <0.01) ( Martin, 2011 ) and reads <15-nt after trimming were discarded. To minimize mismapping, we used a sequential mapping strategy. First, reads were mapped to the human mitochondrial genome (Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93) and the Escherichia coli genome (GeneBank: NC_000913) using HISAT2 v2.1.0 ( Kim et al., 2019 ) with customized settings (-k 10 --rfg 1,3 --rdg 1,3 --mp 4,2 --no-mixed --no-discordant -- no-spliced-alignment) to filter out reads derived from mitochondrial and E. coli RNAs (denoted Pass 1). Unmapped read from Pass1 were then mapped to a collection of customized references sequences for human sncRNAs (miRNA, tRNA, Y RNA, Vault RNA, 7SL RNA, 7SK RNA) and rRNAs (2.2-kb 5S rRNA repeats from the 5S rRNA cluster on chromosome 1 (1q42, Gene- Bank: X12811) and 43-kb 45S rRNA containing 5.8S, 18S and 28S rRNAs from clusters on chromosomes 13,14,15, 21, and 22 (GeneBank: U13369), using HISAT2 with the following settings -k 20 --rdg 1,3 --rfg 1,3 --mp 2,1 --no-mixed --no-discordant --no-spliced-alignment --norc (denoted Pass 2). Unmapped reads from Pass 2 were then mapped to the human genome reference sequence (Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93) using HISAT2 with settings optimized for nonspliced mapping (-k 10 --rdg 1,3 --rfg 1,3 --mp 4,2 --no-mixed --no-discordant --no-spliced- alignment) (denoted Pass 3) followed by splice aware mapping (-k 10 --rdg 1,3 --rfg 1,3 --mp 4,2 --no-mixed --no-discordant --dta) (denoted Pass 4). Finally, the remaining unmapped reads were mapped to Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93 by Bowtie 2 v2.2.5 ( Langmead and Salzberg, 2012 ) using local alignment (-k 10 --rdg 1,3 --rfg 1,3 --mp 4 --ma 1 --no-mixed --no-discordant --very- sensitive-local) to improve mapping rates for reads containing post-transcriptionally added 5’ or 3’ nucleotides (poly(A) or poly(U)), short untrimmed adapter sequences, and non-templated nucleotides added to the 3’ end of the cDNAs by TGIRT-III during TGIRT-seq library preparation (denoted Pass 5). For reads that map to multiple genomic loci with the same mapping score in passes 3 to 5, the alignment with the shortest distance between the two paired ends (i.e., the shortest read span) was selected. In the case of ties (i.e., reads with the same mapping score and read span), reads mapping to a chromosome were selected over reads mapping to scaffold sequences, and in other cases, the read was assigned randomly to one of the tied choices. The filtered multiply mapped reads were then combined with the uniquely mapped reads from Passes 3- 5 by using SAMtools v1.10 ( Li et al., 2009 ).

To generate counts for different genomic features, the mapped reads were intersected with Ensembl GRCh38 Release 93 gene annotations plus the RNY5 gene and its 10 pseudogenes, which are not annotated in this release. Coverage of each feature was calculated by BEDTools v2.29.2 ( Quinlan, 2014 ). To avoid miscounting reads with embedded sncRNAs that were not filtered out in Pass 2 (e.g., snoRNAs), reads were first intersected with sncRNA annotations and the remaining reads were then intersected with the annotations for protein-coding genes RNAs, lincRNAs, antisense RNAs, and other lncRNAs to get the read count for each annotated feature. Coverage plots and read alignments were created by using Integrative Genomics Viewer v2.6.2 (IGV) ( Robinson et al., 2011 ). Genes with >100 mapped reads were down sampled to 100 mapped reads in IGV for visualization.

FLEXI identification and characterization

To identify short introns that could give rise to FLEXI RNAs, intron annotations were extracted from Ensemble GRCh38 Release 93 gene annotations using a customized script and filtered to remove introns >300 nt as well as duplicate intron annotations from different mRNA isoforms. To identify FLEXI RNAs, mapped reads were intersected with the short intron annotations using BEDTools, and read pairs (Read 1 and Read 2) ending at or within 3 nucleotides of annotated 5’- and 3’-splice sites were identified as corresponding to FLEXI RNAs. Exon-junction reads were defined as those in which one of the paired-end read mapped within the intron and the other within a flanking exon.

UpSet plots of FLEXI RNAs from different sample types were plotted by using the ComplexHeatmap package v2.2.0 in R ( Gu et al., 2016 ). For UpSet plots of FLEXI host genes, FLEXI RNAs were aggregated by Ensemble ID, and different FLEXI RNAs from the same gene were combined into one entry. Density distribution plots and scatter plots were plotted by using R.

FLEXI RNAs corresponding to annotated mirtrons or agotrons were identified by intersecting FLEXI RNA coordinates with those of annotated mirtrons ( Wen et al., 2015 ) and agotrons ( Hansen et al., 2016 ). FLEXI RNAs containing embedded snoRNAs were identified by intersecting the FLEXI RNA coordinates with those of annotated snoRNA and scaRNA from Ensembl GRCh38 annotations.

5’- and 3’-splice sites (SS) and branch-point (BP) consensus sequences of human U2- and U12-type spliceosomal introns were obtained from previous publications ( Gao et al., 2008 ; Sheth et al., 2006 ). FLEXI RNAs corresponding to U12-type introns were identified by searching for: (i) FLEXI RNAs with AU-AC ends and (ii) the 5’-splice site consensus sequence of U12-type introns with GU-AG ends ( Sheth et al., 2006 ) using FIMO ( Grant et al., 2011 ) with the following settings: FIMO --text --norc . Splice-site consensus sequences of FLEXI RNAs were calculated from nucleotides frequencies at the 5’ and 3’ ends of the introns. The U2-type intron branch-point consensus sequence of intron RNAs was identified by searching for U2-type branch-point consensus using FIMO ( Grant et al., 2011 ). The U12-type intron branch-point consensus was determined by searching for motifs enriched within 40 nt of the 3’ end of the introns using MEME ( Bailey et al., 2009 ) with settings: meme -rna -oc -mod anr -nmotifs 100 -minw 6 -minsites 100 -markov order 1 -evt 0.05. The branch-point consensus sequence of U12-type FLEXI RNAs (2 with AU-AC ends and 34 with GU-AG matching the 5’ sequence of GU-AG U12-type introns) was determined by manual sequence alignment and calculation of nucleotide frequencies. Motif logos were plotted from the nucleotide frequency tables of each motif using scripts from MEME suite ( Bailey et al., 2009 ).

Clustering analysis

PCA analysis of cell-type specific FLEXI RNA profiles in replicate cellular RNA datasets was plotted using R, and PCA initialized t-SNE and ZINB-WaVE analyses of these datasets were plotted using the Rtsne ( https://github.com/jkrijthe/Rtsne ) and zinbwave ( https://github.com/drisso/zinbwave ) packages in R ( Risso et al., 2018 ). Batch effect correction was done by using ZINB-WaVE to include the batch information as a sample-level covariate. The normalized counts outputted by ZINB-WaVE were used for PCA analysis, and the W matrix outputted by ZINB-WaVE was used for visualization directly or by t-SNE.

RBP-binding site analysis

FLEXI RNAs containing RBP-binding sites were identified by intersecting the FLEXI RNA coordinates with ENCODE annotated binding sites of 150 RBPs from the eCLIP-seq with IDR analysis ( Van Nostrand et al., 2016 ) and crosslink-centered regions (CCRs) identified from the DICER PAR-CLIP ( Rybak-Wolf et al., 2014 ) and AGO1-4 PAR-CLIP ( Hafner et al., 2010 ) datasets by using BEDTools. Multiple binding sites for the same RBP in an intron were collapsed and counted as one site for that RBP. Functional annotations, localization patterns, and predicted RNA-binding domains of RBPs in the ENCODE eCLIP dataset were based on Table S2 of ( Van Nostrand et al., 2020 ). RBPs found in stress granules were as annotated in the RNA Granule and Mammalian Stress Granules Proteome (MSGP) databases ( Nunes et al., 2019 ; Youn et al., 2019 ). The functional annotations, localization patterns, and RNA-binding domains of AGO1-4 and DICER were from the UniProt database ( The UniProt Consortium, 2018 ). Near full length FLEXI or other short intron reads were identified in ENCODE eCLIP datasets by intersecting mapped eCLIP reads with FLEXI or other short intron coordinates using BedTools.

Gene expression changes in RBP knockdown datasets were calculated from changes in mRNA abundance measured by using DESeq2 after GC correction with Salmon ( Patro et al., 2017 ) and CQN ( Hansen et al., 2012 ). The number of significant gene expression changes (DESeq2-calculated adjusted p-value<0.05) for FLEXI host genes was compared to three gene sets containing the same number of randomly selected genes, and ANOVA was used to identify knockdowns that resulted in significantly more gene expression changes among FLEXI host genes than expected by random chance. To identify FLEXI host genes whose changes in mRNA levels were significantly biased in one direction, the numbers of upregulated and downregulated host genes containing a FLEXI with a binding site for the knocked-down RBP (defined as log 2 - transformed fold change>0, adjusted p-value<0.05) were compared to those for all significantly changed genes by Fisher’s exact test, and those with p-value <0.05 for difference in one or the other direction were considered significantly biased in that direction. Volcano plots were generating by plotting log 2 transformed fold changes in the mRNA levels between the knockdown and control datasets versus -log 10 adjusted p-values.

Changes in alternative splicing were calculated by using rMATS ( Shen et al., 2014 ). Coordinates of adjacent introns or of retained introns from splicing change files (MATS output) were intersected with coordinates of bound FLEXIs (i.e., FLEXIs that contain a binding site for a RBP), unbound FLEXIs (i.e., FLEXIs that do not contain a binding site for the same RBP), and other short introns, and the results were displayed as a density plot (Seaborn distplot) showing the inclusion level difference for the same intron between the knockdown and control datatsets.

To map RBP-binding sites within introns, RBP-binding sites from ENCODE eCLIP datasets were intersected with FLEXI, other short intron, or long intron coordinates (BedTools). Any binding site overlapping with any portion of the intron was used. The location of the binding site with respect to normalized intron length was calculated from the distance between the mid-point of the annotated binding site and the beginning or end of the intron. Data were displayed as a density plot of the mid-point of the binding sites normalized by intron length.

For hierarchical clustering analysis of the RBP-binding site co-enrichment, we first extracted subsets of FLEXIs that have a binding site for each of 47 RBPs that have binding sites in ≥30 different FLEXI RNAs from datasets for each of the cellular RNA samples. We then identified over- and under-represented RBPs in each of the 47 subsets for each cellular RNA sample by generating a contingency table comparing the frequency of binding sites for the 126 RBPs that have an identified binding site in a FLEXI RNA ( Fig. S9 ) in the tested subset to its frequency in all FLEXIs. Multiple binding sites for the same RBP in the same FLEXI were counted as one binding site. p-values were calculated using Fisher’s exact test and then adjusted by the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure and log 10 transformed. RBP binding sites were then key-coded as not significantly different or as significantly over- or under-represented (≥2% abundance, p≤0.05) in the tested subset of FLEXIs compared to all FLEXIs, and matrices of the Gower’s distance ( Gower, 1971 ) were calculated using the key-coded information from the above analyses and used as input for hierarchical clustering by the complete linkage clustering method ( Defays, 1977 ). The results were displayed as a heat map plotted using the R package cluster and ComplexHeatmap, respectively.

GO enrichment analysis of host genes encoding FLEXIs bound by different RBPs was performed by using DAVID bioinformatics tools ( Huang et al., 2009 ) with all FLEXI host genes as the background. Hierarchical clustering was performed based on p-values for GO term enrichment of FLEXI host genes bound by the same RBPs using the Seaborn ClusterMap package in Python ( https://zenodo.org ).
