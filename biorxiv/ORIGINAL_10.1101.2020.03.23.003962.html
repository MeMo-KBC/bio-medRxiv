Abstract

Biochemical signaling networks allow living cells to adapt to a changing environment, but these networks must cope with unavoidable number fluctuations (“noise”) in their molecular constituents. Escherichia coli chemotaxis, by which bacteria modulate their random run/tumble swimming pattern to navigate their environment, is a paradigm for the role of noise in cell signaling. The key signaling protein, CheY, when activated by (reversible) phosphorylation, causes a switch in the rotational direction of the flagellar motors propelling the cell, leading to tumbling. CheY-P concentration, [CheY-P], is thus a measure of the chemotaxis network’s output, and temporal fluctuations in [CheY-P] provide a proxy for network noise. Here we quantify the fluctuations in [CheY-P] from the switching dynamics of individual flagella, observed using time-resolved fluorescence microscopy of optically trapped E. coli cells. This approach reveals [CheY-P] fluctuations at steady state, which may play a critical role in driving flagellar switching and cell tumbling. A stochastic theoretical model, inspired by work on gene expression noise, points to CheY activation occurring in bursts, driving the [CheY-P] fluctuations. When the network is stimulated chemically to higher activity, we observe a dramatic decrease in [CheY-P] fluctuations. Our stochastic model shows that an intrinsic kinetic ceiling on network activity places an upper limit on [CheY-P], which when encountered suppresses its fluctuations. This limit may also prevent cells from tumbling unproductively in steep gradients.

Introduction

A common feature of living organisms is their ability to sense environmental signals and respond to these signals by modifying their behavior. This ability is enabled by signal transduction, which converts environmental inputs into behavioral outputs 1 . Studies have shown that signaling networks must operate in the presence of noise not only in their inputs but also in the circuit components themselves 2 , 3 . Until recently, it was thought that signaling networks had evolved to be robust against undesirable noise 4 – 7 . More recent studies have shifted to understanding the role of noise in regulating and fine-tuning biological function 8 , 9 . For example, noise resulting from transcription/translation has been measured precisely and shown to play a critical role in networks for gene expression and regulation 10 – 12 . In contrast, studying noise resulting from signaling networks at the post-translation level has proven more challenging.

One extensively studied signaling system is the chemotaxis network of E. coli, which cells use to navigate their environment 13 – 17 . E. coli cells swim in a random walk consisting of “runs”— during which their flagella rotate counter-clockwise (CCW)—and “tumbles”—during which one or more flagella rotate clockwise (CW) 18 – 21 . Changing environmental conditions are sensed through a two-component signaling system comprising receptors that bind extracellular ligands and a kinase, CheA, that transfers its phosphoryl group onto downstream effectors 22 . The response regulator, CheY, when phosphorylated by the receptor kinase complex to CheY-P, binds to the flagellar motor and increases the probability of CW motor rotation 23 – 25 . The phosphatase CheZ carries out the opposite reaction, maintaining a dynamic equilibrium between CheY and CheY-P ( Figure 1a ) 26 , 27 . Additionally, the methyltransferase CheR and the methylesterase CheB covalently modify receptors, and the resulting modulation of CheA activity affects CheY-P levels over the timescales of chemotactic adaptation 28 – 30 . While the population averaged relationships between these signaling protein concentrations and cellular motility in E. coli are now well understood 13 – 16 , 24 , the role of fluctuations in protein concentrations in modulating cell behavior remains comparatively unexplored.

The intracellular CheY-P concentration, [CheY-P], is a dynamic indicator of the cell’s environment and determines its trajectory of motion. Thus, [CheY-P] fluctuations are the primary indicator of noise upstream in the signaling network and affect downstream behavior. Cluzel et al. 25 showed that the motor CW bias, the probability of CW rotation of a single flagellar motor, has an ultrasensitive switch-like dependence on the intracellular [CheY-P]. Thus, the cell’s swimming behavior is sensitive to small temporal fluctuations in [CheY-P] ( Figure 1b, i-iii ). Noise due to CheY-P fluctuations was first inferred by Korobkova et al. 31 from long-term measurements of individual rotating flagellar motors, where larger-than-expected noise in the motor CW bias was attributed to methylation/demethylation dynamics of CheR/CheB over long timescales (10s of seconds). Recently, single-cell measurements probing intracellular [CheY-P] using fluorescent reporters revealed a potential new source of noise arising from the dynamics of the receptor kinase complex itself 32 , 33 . Noise in the E. coli chemotaxis signaling network is thought to have functional consequences for sensing and navigation in complex natural microenvironments. It has been proposed to allow cells to sample 3D space more effectively 31 , 34 , enhance chemotactic drift up a gradient 35 – 37 , and synchronize flagellar switching to mitigate differences in swimming behavior among cells with different numbers of flagella 38 , 39 .

Here, we use optical trapping coupled with time-resolved fluorescence microscopy 39 , 40 to infer temporal [CheY-P] fluctuations from the statistics of flagellar switching in individual multi-flagellated E. coli cells. In contrast to fluorescence-based approaches that provide a relative measure of noise 32 , 33 , here it is measured directly in terms of CheY-P concentration fluctuations. Analyzing cells at steady state, we determine the single-cell distribution of [CheY-P], which reveals that the network is poised at a low CW bias, favoring CCW flagellar rotation/running, and that transient fluctuations in [CheY-P] drive CW flagellar rotation/tumbling. Unlike previous studies where fluctuations were studied over long timescales (>10 s) 31 – 33 , 41 , our method measures fluctuations at the timescales of individual runs and tumbles (0.5-5 s), which allows us to track the time evolution of signaling noise in cells undergoing adaptation. We find that fluctuations are significantly reduced upon applying a stimulus that increases CheA activity. A simple stochastic model in which noise in signaling arises from large, burst-like fluctuations in CheA activity recapitulates all of our experimental results and suggests that the decrease in CheY-P fluctuations upon stimulation is due to a kinetic ceiling on the CheA activity.

Results

Flagellar switching statistics reveal temporal [CheY-P] fluctuations

We inferred the temporal fluctuations in the intracellular CheY-P concentration from the dynamics of flagellar switching in individual cells. We imaged fluorescently labeled flagella on individual, swimming cells to determine their CCW/CW rotation state, using an optical trapping assay described previously 39 , 40 , 42 (see Materials and Methods ). Briefly, E. coli cells were injected into a laminar flow chamber containing two parallel channels. A single cell was captured using a dual beam optical trap from one channel containing many cells (labeled “cells” in Figure 1c ) and moved for observation to a second channel containing no other cells (labeled “blank” in Figure 1c ). The light scattered by the optically trapped cell was used to monitor the run/tumble swimming behavior as described previously (data not shown, see Ref. [40]). Simultaneously, high-speed, epi-fluorescent stroboscopic imaging 39 was used to monitor the rotation state of all the individual flagella on the cell at high temporal resolution (0.1 s) and long-time duration (typically ~15-40 s, until the flagella photobleached) ( Figure 1d , top). Following Darnton et al. 21 and our previous work 39 , the CCW/CW rotation state of each flagellum was visually identified by its different helical waveforms (see Materials and Methods ).

Thus, one expects that the distribution p(c), and thus the distribution p(y P ) which captures fluctuations in [CheY-P], can be estimated over a given time window with increasing accuracy as N increases. Essentially, since each flagellar motor independently measures the intracellular level of CheY-P, the more motors a cell possesses, the better our estimate of the mean [CheY-P] and its fluctuations.

We note that while p(c) is determined exactly from Eq. (1) with no additional input parameters, p(y P ) requires values for the parameters H, K, and y tot in Eqs. (4) and ( 5 ). In particular, p(y P ) depends non-negligibly on K and H (see Appendix for a detailed discussion on the sensitivity to K and H). Initial single-cell measurements of the motor c vs. y P response function 25 yielded values of H = 10.3 and K = 3.1 μM. However, the discovery of remodeling in the flagellar motor 45 – 47 —which causes the number of FliM, the component of the motor switch complex that binds CheY-P, to vary according to intracellular CheY-P levels—has led to revised estimates of the Hill coefficient, H ~ 20, nearly twice as large 47 . Furthermore, remodeling causes the motor response function to shift depending on CheY-P level, such that K (and to a lesser extent H) depend on the steady-state CW bias of the motor 43 , 45 (see Appendix for a discussion on remodeling). As detailed below, we account for the effect of motor remodeling in determining the appropriate values of K and H in Eqs. (4) and ( 5 ) given the CW bias of the cell. We also note that, since y P is determined only relative to K, we plot and report values for the ratio yp/K, which is the functionally important parameter for E. coli chemotaxis. In contrast to K and H, p(y P ) is insensitive to the exact value of y tot , because the CW bias c ≈ 1 when y P = y tot > K according to Eq. (3) . (The upper limit for the integral in Eq. (4) can be replaced with ∞ with error <0.01%). Lastly we note that writing Eqs. (4) and ( 5 ) implicitly assumes that the motor response function, c(y P ), is identical for all motors. An analysis of the CW bias across motors and cells indicates that this variability is negligible, and justifies our use of a single value for K and for H across all cells (see Appendix for further discussion).

The chemotaxis network exhibits [CheY-P] fluctuations at steady-state

We first applied the above approach to free-swimming (i.e. unstimulated) cells for which the chemotaxis network is in a steady state. Fluorescence tracking data of CCW/CW flagellar states were obtained for cells of a strain wild-type for chemotaxis (HCB1660, henceforth referred to as “wild-type”; see Materials and Methods ) with N = 2 to 5 flagella. Data traces tracking n cw were pooled together from multiple cells with the same total number of flagella N to generate p N (n cw ) ( Figure 2a , colored histograms). We carried out several tests to confirm that the width of the estimated [CheY-P] distribution resulted from noise in signal transduction. First, we applied our method of analysis to a strain that expresses the constitutively active mutant protein CheY D13K , a mimic for CheY-P 24 (strain PM87, henceforth referred to as “CheY*”; see Materials and Methods ). Since CheY in this strain is decoupled from the upstream network components and the concentration of CheY D13K is determined from gene expression levels, which should not fluctuate on the timescales of our experiments in nutrient-limited conditions 48 , we expected to observe smaller fluctuations in [CheY D13K ] as compared to the wild-type strain. Repeating the above analysis, Figures 2d-f show the experimental distributions p N (n cw ) along with all fit distributions. Here, the CW bias and FliM dynamics model yielded parameter values K = 2.5 μM and H = 18, similar to those for wild-type cells, which were used to determine the [CheY D13K ] distribution. Values for the mean [CheY D13K ] and standard deviation relative to K from fits for each N and from a global fit are found in Table 5 . The mean [CheY-P] relative to K is similar for both strains, and, as expected, fluctuations in the CheY* strain are much reduced compared to the wild-type strain, yielding a coefficient of variation of 0.1.

Second, we tested whether the width of the estimated [CheY-P] distribution could be due to cell-to-cell differences in mean [CheY-P] rather than fluctuations arising in signal transduction. To test the contribution of cell-to-cell variation, we determined the best fit [CheY-P] distributions for individual cell traces ( Figure 3 – figure supplement 1 and figure supplement 2 ) and compared the wild-type and CheY* strains. As shown in Figure 3a and 3b , while the mean [CheY-P] relative to K, μ y p /K, from individual cell distributions was similar for both strains, the coefficient of variation, σ y p /μ y p , was significantly smaller for the CheY* strain as compared to the wild-type strain (p ~ 10 -4 ), as expected. Importantly, the coefficient of variation for individual wild-type cells averaged over N, <σ y p /μ y p > ≈ 0.24, was close to that determined over the population. This indicates that cell-to-cell variation over the wild-type cell population does not contribute greatly to the observed width in the [CheY-P] distribution. In contrast, <σy*/μy*> was significantly smaller for individual cells of the CheY* strain, ~0.05, as compared to the population, 0.1. We suspect cell-to-cell CheY D13K expression differences may contribute to the estimated distributions in Figure 2d-f . Nevertheless, our results show that we can attribute the width in the [CheY-P] distribution for wild-type cells to fluctuations in [CheY-P] due to signaling activity. Our estimate of CheY-P fluctuations in wild-type cells is consistent with a theoretically predicted lower bound of 20% of the mean 49 .

Our method for determining [CheY-P] fluctuations in terms of the functional [CheY-P] concentration allows us to make one interesting observation. Comparing our estimated distribution to the Hill curve relating CW bias to [CheY-P], Eq. (3) ( Figure 2c ), we notice that the peak of the distribution resides at low CW biases, c < 0.05, corresponding to CCW flagellar rotation and running a majority of the time. CW rotation and tumbling thus result from fluctuations away from the peak, in the tail of the distribution at higher [CheY-P]. This finding contrasts with the conventional view that the network is poised near the midpoint of the Hill curve 25 , 36 , i.e. with p(y P ) sharply peaked near [CheY-P] ≈ K, and suggests that fluctuations are a critical determinant of the tumbling frequency.

[CheY-P] fluctuations decrease upon stimulating the network to increase CW bias

Having characterized fluctuations in the chemotactic network in the steady state, we next investigated them in a network stimulated by a perturbation. In response to a stepwise change in chemical environment, the intracellular [CheY-P] changes rapidly, and then reverts to its steady-state value through adaptation 50 – 52 . Applying a large stepwise decrease in attractant leads to a transient increase in [CheY-P] and flagellar motor CW bias followed by a decrease to the steady state 42 , 53 , allowing us to measure network fluctuations across the sensitive range of the motor CW bias vs. [CheY-P] curve, Eq. (3) .

We measured the response of individual cells to a decrease in concentration of α-methyl-DL-aspartate (MeAsp), a non-metabolizable analog of L-aspartate 54 that acts as a chemoattractant 54 , 55 . We used a three-channel flow chamber ( Figure 4a ) to observe the effect of a step-down from 1 to 0 mM MeAsp on individual optically trapped wild-type cells. The step-down was applied by quickly moving a trapped cell from the middle channel of the flow chamber containing 1 mM MeAsp ( Figure 4a ; labeled “blank + attractant”) to the top channel containing no MeAsp (labeled “blank”; see Materials and Methods ). The run-tumble behavior of the cell was monitored pre- and post-stimulus via the optical traps (see Materials and Methods ), whereas the flagellar rotation state was measured by fluorescence imaging only post-stimulus.

Fundamentally, the low number fluctuations predicted from Eq. (7) are due to the large total number (~8,000) of CheY molecules in the cell. Matching our experimental data would require decreasing the number of CheY by over two orders of magnitude (equivalent to replacing V C with a cell volume two orders of magnitude smaller in Eq. Although a recent study proposed that CheY sequestration at the cell poles where many signaling proteins reside may limit the number of CheY-P participating in signaling 61 , such a mechanism is unlikely to account for this discrepancy between Eq. Second, the dramatic decrease in coefficient of variation near , which results from the observed narrowing of the [CheY-P] distributions in stimulated cells ( Figure 4e ), is not recapitulated in the model. Eq. (7) predicts a drop to zero at μ y p ≈ 10 μM or , when all of the CheY becomes phosphorylated. One possibility to account for these observations is a ceiling of on the amount of CheY available for phosphorylation, leading to a maximum relative [CheY-P] ≈ 1.2 and a reduction in the width of the [CheY-P] distribution at this concentration. To test this mechanism, we constructed a mutant ΔcheZ strain (“RB03”; see Materials and Methods ) lacking the phosphatase CheZ, in which all the available CheY is expected to be phosphorylated. We replicated our flagellar imaging analysis and calculated the [CheY-P] distribution for this strain, assuming the same values for K and H as for wild type cells (2.4 μM and 18, respectively). This yielded a mean [CheY-P]/K of 1.6 ± 0.1, corresponding to a CW bias close to 1 according to Eq. (3) (data not shown), higher than that observed following a step-down stimulus, ruling out such a mechanism. This result holds when we account for the effect of motor remodeling in this strain, which manifests as an increase in K compared to the wild type strain, since the mean [CheY-P] scales with K.

Discussion

In this work we demonstrate a new approach for inferring fluctuations in [CheY-P] based on the statistics of flagellar switching in multi-flagellated E. coli cells. One advantage of this approach is that its accuracy in quantifying network noise does not depend on temporal averaging, which limited previous measurements to long time scales (>10 s) 31 , 33 , but instead relies on multiple flagellar motors making independent simultaneous “measurements” of intracellular CheY-P levels. This feature allows us to measure [CheY-P] noise at short timescales (0.5-5 s) and to track the time evolution of signaling noise in cells as they undergo adaptation.

Another advantage of this method is that noise parameters are determined in terms of CheY-P concentration (relative to K), which allows for direct comparison to theoretical models. In contrast, prior fluorescence-based studies determine changes in CheA activity relative to a baseline 32 , 33 . However, one limit is that our estimate of p(y P ) depends on the relation between CW bias and [CheY-P], parametrized by K and H in the Hill curve, Eq. (3) . As discussed above, we consider uncertainties and variations in these parameters in our analysis. It is important to highlight that the relative differences between various [CheY-P] distributions—such as between different time points in adapting cells—still remain significant (see Appendix – figure 1 ). Thus, the trends in our results remain valid independent of parameter values.

Previous single-cell measurements of flagellar rotation by bead tracking 31 , 41 and of CheA activity using fluorescent reporters 32 , 33 have reported long-term noise in the chemotaxis network attributed to CheR-CheB methylation-demethylation dynamics 31 and, recently, new sources such as receptor clustering and other dynamics 32 , 33 . A direct comparison between these results and ours is difficult because of the differences in timescales between the measurements (0.1-40 s vs. 10-1000 s). While our approach extends to fast timescales, photobleaching of the dyes limits the duration of our traces and limits the overlap between the measurement timescales. Nevertheless, to attempt such a comparison, we determined how our estimate of CheY-P noise varies with the measurement time window. As shown in Figure 5 – figure supplement 1 , the noise increases with longer windows. This trend is consistent with our short-time measurements capturing the tail of a longer-timescale noise source. We are unable to determine the timescale precisely, but estimate that it must be >10 s, consistent with reported values of ~13 s 32 , 33 . Thus, it is plausible that these different measurements are consistent and that source of CheY-P noise are the same. Nevertheless, we have deliberately made our model for CheA activity fluctuations in Eq. (8) agnostic to the source of noise to allow for alternatives.

The noise characteristics of the chemotaxis network revealed in our measurements may have functional consequences. Our findings suggest that [CheY-P] fluctuations are critical drivers for CW flagellar rotation/tumbling in E. coli. Our experimentally determined mean [CheY-P]/K of 0.8 restricts the cell to a CW bias <0.05, corresponding to running the majority of the time. Previously, Terasawa et al. 38 proposed that waves of CheA activity could cause transient increases in [CheY-P]. Our data and model appear to be consistent with this mechanism, with the “burst size” b in Eq. (8) representing such waves in activity. Our fit parameters indicate that ~3.5% of all CheY are phosphorylated during such events. We envision that transient increases in [CheY-P], driven by waves of phosphorylation by the receptor kinase complex 38 , increases the CW bias to generate tumbles. In previous work, it was proposed that [CheY-P] fluctuations act to synchronize flagellar switching 38 , 39 , which was shown to make swimming behavior robust to variation in flagella number 39 . E. coli has also been proposed to exploit long-timescale [CheY-P] fluctuations to explore larger volumes of space 31 , 34 and to increase drift speeds, enhancing migration along increasing attractant gradients 35 – 37 . Lastly, the kinetic ceiling in receptor kinase activity which restricts CheY-P fluctuations when the mean [CheY-P] increases could prevent CheY-P concentrations from saturating and leading to continuous and unproductive CW flagellar rotation/tumbling. Such behavior could result in increased E. coli swimming efficiency down steep decreases in attractant concentration, as likely found in natural environments.

Materials and Methods

Microbiology

Cell preparation

Experiments were performed on the HCB1660 E. coli strain, a gift from the Berg lab 66 . This strain is considered wild-type for chemotaxis but is ΔfliC expressing FliC S219C under the control of the ParaBAD promoter. The mutant FliC S219C protein was specifically labelled with a cysteine reactive fluorescent dye, Alexa Fluor 532 C5 Maleimide (A10255, ThermoFisher Scientific) using the method first described by Turner et al. 66 and later used by Mears et al. 39 . The strain referred to as CheY* is PM87, ΔfliC ΔcheBYZ expressing FliC S219C and constitutively active CheY D13K , constructed by Mears et al. 39 . The strain referred to as RB03 (constructed for this work) is ΔfliC ΔcheZ expressing FliC S219C .

As described by Mears et al. 39 , for each experiment, a single colony from an LB, lysogeny broth, agar plate was inoculated into 1 mL tryptone broth (TB: 0.8% [wt/vol] NaCl, 1% Bacto Tryptone), and grown to saturation overnight (14-18 hours), shaking at 265 RPM at 30°C with the appropriate antibiotics. The overnight culture was diluted 100-fold into 12 mL TB, and grown to OD600 ~0.4 to 0.5, with 0.01% [wt/vol] or 666 μM L-arabinose, shaking at 265 RPM at 30°C for ~4.5 hours. The over-day culture was washed twice by slow centrifugation (1300 × g) and gently resuspended in 1 mL motility buffer (MB: 10 mM Phosphate Buffer [pH 7.0], 70 mM NaCl and 0.1 mM EDTA) 21 .

To label flagella fluorescently, we followed the protocol reported by Mears et al. 39 . An appropriate volume of the Alexa Fluor 532 C5 Maleimide dye was added to resuspended cells in 0.5 mL MB to a concentration of ~0.01 mg/mL and incubated with slow rotation (~10 RPM) at room temperature in the dark for 90 min. The labeled cells were gently resuspended in 1 mL MB. For trapping, cells were diluted 20-fold in trap motility buffer (TMB: 70 mM NaCl, 100 mM Tris-Cl, 2% [wt/vol] glucose, with 0.1 mM methionine and an oxygen-scavenging system [290 μg ml -1 pyranose oxidase and 65 μg ml -1 catalase] 40 ) and injected into the flow chamber for the trapping experiment. When handling resuspended cells, at all times, we avoided pipetting, or we used wide orifice pipette tips to avoid shearing flagella.

1 mg of the dry Alexa Fluor 532 C5 Maleimide dye was dissolved in 250 μL water by vortexing and stored in aliquots at −20°C. The stock concentration for each aliquot was determined using UV-Visible Absorption Spectroscopy. Since this dye does not dissolve readily in water, we found it important to determine the concentration for each aliquot separately for optimal labeling.

Data Acquisition

Optical trapping of individual E. coli with simultaneous fluorescence imaging

Experiments were performed with a dual-trap optical tweezers instrument combined with epi-fluorescence slim-field imaging, developed and described in detail in previous work by Mears et al. 39 . Briefly, the optical traps were created from a single 5-W, 1064-nm laser (YLR-5-1064-LP, IPG Photonics). Two trapping beams were generated by timesharing, intermittently deflecting the input beam between two positions with an Acousto-Optic Deflector, AOD (DTSXY-250-1064, AA Opto-Electronic). The two resulting beams were then tightly focused using a 60X, water immersion (1.2 NA) objective (Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) to create two optical traps. An identical objective was used to collect the transmitted trap light for position detection and for bright-field imaging of the cells using Koehler illumination from a blue LED. A bacterial cell could be captured and aligned between the two optical traps without impeding its rotatory motion. The light scattered by the trapped cell was collected onto a position sensitive photodetector and used to monitor cell motion, which was analyzed to determine runs and tumbles as described below and previously by Min et al. 40 .

Fluorescently labeled flagella of trapped cells were imaged using a 532-nm laser (TECGL-30, World Star Tech) aligned for slim-field illumination 68 , with the excitation beam diameter at the sample plane ~10-15 μm. We strobed the fluorescence excitation and trap laser illumination out of phase with each other to minimize photobleaching 39 . The fluorescence illumination was strobed by using an Acousto-Optic Modulator, AOM (802AF1, IntraAction) to deflect the excitation light intermittently into the sample plane for a duration of 16-20 μs each cycle while the traps were off. An EMCCD camera (iXon3 860 EMCCD, Andor) recorded images in synchrony with the excitation pulse at the rate of 100 frames per second to capture focused images of the flagella as they rotated at a rate of ~100 Hz. In this manner, movies of flagellar motion were recorded, typically for ~30-40 s until the flagella photobleached. Movies were saved using the Solis software (Andor) and processed using custom written code in MATLAB. Each movie was analyzed manually to count flagella and their transitions between CW and CCW rotating states.

Motility assays combined with fluorescence imaging in trapped cells

Custom-built microfluidic chambers shown in Figure 1c and 4a (preparation described in detail in Mears et al. 39 and Min et al. 42 ) were used to perform motility assays coupled with fluorescence imaging of labeled flagella on individual trapped cells. To prepare chambers, glass coverslips were sonicated in acetone, then rinsed in deionized water. This step was followed by rinsing in methanol and drying under nitrogen flow. The flow channels in the chamber were cut out from Nescofilm (Alfresa Pharma Corporation, Osaka, Japan) and bonded to glass coverslips as described previously 39 , 42 .

For measurements of free-swimming, or unstimulated, cells, the chamber contained two channels ( Figure 1c ). The upper channel contained trap motility buffer (TMB) only, while the lower channel contained E. coli cells in TMB. Both channels were continuously injected with the appropriate solutions using a syringe pump (PHD2000, Harvard Apparatus) with a linear flow rate of ~30 μm/s. Cells were trapped in the lower channel and moved to the upper channel by displacing the chamber with respect to the traps using a motorized three-axis translational stage (ESP301; Newport, Irvine, California). Once the trapped cell was aligned between the two traps in the upper channel, the bright-field illumination was turned off and the 532-nm excitation illumination was turned on. Cell motion was observed by simultaneously recording the optical trap signal sensed by position sensitive photodetectors and the fluorescence signal from labelled flagella sensed by the EMCCD camera.

For measurements of stimulated cells undergoing chemotactic adaptation, the chamber contained three channels ( Figure 4a ). For a step-down stimulus, the top (“blank”) channel contained TMB only, the middle (“blank + attractant”) channel contained TMB, 1 mM α-methyl-DL-aspartate (MeAsp) and Rhodamine B (100 nM), and the bottom (“cell + attractant”) channel contained TMB, 1 mM MeAsp and E. coli cells. Fluorescence imaging of the Rhodamine B diffusion profile was used to characterize the gradient formed across the top and the middle channel, as described by Min et al. 42 . All three channels of the flow chamber were injected with their solutions at a linear flow rate of ~50 μm/s. In a typical experiment, a swimming cell was first captured from the “cells + attractant” channel. The trapped cell was moved to the “blank + attractant” channel, aligned between the two traps, and oriented along the direction of the flow. Pre-stimulus optical trap signal of the swimming cell was recorded for ~200 s (without any fluorescence imaging of flagella). The trapped cell was then moved at the speed 100 μm/s to the “blank” channel so that it experienced a step down in MeAsp concentration over a ~4-s duration (as shown in Figure S1 in Min et al. 42 ). During this move, the bright field illumination was turned off and the 532-nm excitation illumination was turned on. The chemotactic response of the captured cell was recorded in the “blank” channel by simultaneously recording the optical trap signal and the fluorescence signal from labelled flagella. While the trap signal post-stimulus was recorded for ~400 s, the EMCCD signal was recorded only for ~30-40 s until the flagella photobleached.

Raw Data Analysis

Run-tumble analysis for optical trap data

Cell motion was detected from imaging the optical trap light scattered by the cell body onto position-sensitive photodetectors, as described previously by Min et al. 40 . This optical trap signal was analyzed for runs and tumbles. Briefly, runs corresponded to oscillatory signals due to cell body roll at a frequency ~10 Hz, while tumbles corresponded to erratic trap signals with a frequency <1 Hz. We used custom-written MATLAB code incorporating a wavelet analysis to identify the peak body roll frequency at every time point of each data trace and to assign runs and tumbles by specifying an appropriate frequency threshold 40 . An example trace of the trap signal and the corresponding run-tumble assignment is shown in Figure 1c and d in earlier work by Mears et al. 39 .

Image analysis for fluorescence data

Images acquired using the high-speed fluorescence imaging described above were adjusted for contrast to best visualize the flagella. Each movie was played in slow motion and Clockwise (CW)/Counter Clockwise (CCW) states were assigned manually to each flagellum over a time window of 100 ms (i.e. 10 frames). As described previously 21 , 39 , the CCW/CW rotation state of the flagella can be visually identified from their different helical waveforms, commonly referred to as ‘normal’, ‘semi-coiled’, ‘curly-1’ and ‘curly-2’. CCW-rotating flagella exclusively adopt the ‘normal’ waveform and CW-rotating flagella adopt the ‘semi-coiled’ and ‘curly-1’ waveforms the majority of the time. Waveform transitions take less than ~0.5 s to propagate through the flagella.

Custom statistical test

In order to make comparisons between sets of data points each with an error bar (for example, in Figure 3 ), standard statistical tests such as the t-test and Wilcoxon rank sum test could not be used. Hence a custom statistical test based on the permutation test 70 was written in order to make such comparisons. The null hypothesis for this test was that the observed means of the two data sets are equal. First, surrogate data sets were created under the assumption of this null hypothesis. Surrogate data for each data point in the two data sets being compared was created by random sampling from a gamma distribution with mean equal to the value of the data point and the standard deviation equal to the value of the error bar on the data point. Next, the labels on each point in the surrogate data were randomly permuted to create two separate surrogate data sets with the same number of points as the ones being compared. For each such pair of surrogate data sets, a test statistic under the null hypothesis was calculated as the difference of their means. For 50,000 such surrogate data sets, the probability distribution of the test statistic under the null hypothesis was plotted. The P-value for this custom statistical test was calculated as the probability of finding the value of the test statistic, i.e. difference between means under the null hypothesis, that is greater than the observed difference of means.

Effect of flagellar motor remodeling on CheY-P distribution

Studies have shown that the flagellar motor exhibits adaptive remodeling 45 – 47 . FliM, a component of the switch complex at the base of the flagellar motor that binds CheY-P, is dynamic and able to bind to or dissociate from the motor. Effect of motor-to-motor differences on CheY-P distribution

In Eqs. (4) -( 5 ) of the main text, we implicitly assumed that the CW bias vs. CheY-P relation, Eq. (3) , is the same for all motors, i.e. the parameters K and H are constants. However, there may be differences between motors that lead to variations in these parameters. Recent work 71 has shown that the load on the flagellar motors shifts the curve midpoint, K. Thus, differences in load (e.g. from different flagellar lengths) could lead to variation in CW bias across motors for the same intracellular [CheY-P].

To estimate the variation in c vs. y p curves across motors of the same cell, we analyzed time traces from cells of the CheY* strain. Since we could identify each flagellum during our fluorescence movies, we determined the time-averaged CW bias of each motor individually and quantified CW bias differences across motors on the same cell. As CheY* expression is controlled, its concentration is assumed to be constant over the time scale of the measurements (30-40 s) for each individual cell. Effect of cell-to-cell variation on CheY-P distribution

We also explored the effect of variation in the c vs. y P curves between cells. Similarly to above, we estimated variations in the parameter K across a wild-type cell population by determining the time-averaged CW bias of individual cells and quantifying cell-to-cell differences CW bias.
